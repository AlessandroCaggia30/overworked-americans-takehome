\begin{planbox}
\small\textbf{Plan.}
\textbf{Q1} -- the US--Europe hours gap is two distinct facts (Northern intensive regime; Mediterranean+East extensive regime). A simplistic mechanism cannot explain both. Taxes \emph{interacting with} country-specific institutions (filing unit, childcare, informal sector, employment protection) can.
\textbf{Q2} -- three nested closed-form labour-supply models. The synthesis (Cogan 1981 / Heckman 1974 / Chang--Kim 2006) makes the interaction explicit through a logistic-distributed fixed cost of working with country-specific mean $\mu_b^j$ (calibrated to OECD 2023 employment) and common dispersion $\sigma_b\!=\!0.20$. The cross-country regime split is therefore captured by $\mu_b^j$, not by $\sigma_b^j$; the calibration is owned as such (not "fitted to BFS Tab.\ 2") in \S 2.5.
\textbf{Q7} -- ten-channel closed-form welfare ledger with four institutional propositions (P1 hours caps, P2 unions, P3 formal/informal, P4 employment protection). Calibrated to primary sources, validated against Jones--Klenow (2016) within 2 pp on EU cells. Reduced-form Aubry 35-hour DiD ($\hat\beta\!=\!-0.042$, $t\!=\!-6.6$) anchors (P1). Verdict: $\mathcal W^{NW}/\mathcal W^{US}\!=\!1.025$ (NW weakly above US); $\mathcal W^{ME}/\mathcal W^{US}\!=\!0.778$ (Mediterranean dominated). Reproducible: \texttt{research/repl\_q7\_ledger.py}, \texttt{research/repl\_aubry\_did.py}.

\section{Q1 -- Documenting the US--Europe hours gap}
% =========================================================================

\begingroup\small\itshape\noindent\textbf{Question 1.} Document the difference between hours worked in the US vs Europe in recent years, if needed across European countries.\endgroup

\begin{tldr}
\small\textbf{Bottom line.} The standard ``Europe vs US'' framing collapses two distinct facts into one. \emph{Northern Europe} (Germany, Netherlands, Sweden, UK) works less per worker than the US -- the gap is on the \emph{intensive margin}. \emph{Mediterranean+East} (Italy, Spain, Greece, Poland, Hungary) works nearly as many hours per worker as the US but employs a much smaller fraction of the population -- the gap is on the \emph{extensive margin}. The crucial implication for Q2: a tax wedge of $\sim\!55\%$ in Germany and Italy produces a $17\%$ intensive-margin collapse in one country and a $28\%$ extensive-margin collapse in the other. \textbf{No simplistic story can do this. But taxes \emph{interacting with} country-specific institutions (joint vs.\ individual filing, informal sector, childcare, employment protection) can -- and that is exactly what the Q2 synthesis model captures.}
\end{tldr}

\subsection{The fact: cross-section of hours and employment, BBF 2019 panel}

I begin by laying out the raw cross-country evidence. Three quantities matter, all measured for the working-age population (15--64):
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Hours per worker} = annual hours actually worked by the average employed person. This is the \emph{intensive margin} of labour supply.
\item \textbf{Employment rate $E/N$} = fraction of the working-age population that is employed. This is the \emph{extensive margin}.
\item \textbf{Hours per adult $H/N$} = the product, hours per worker $\times$ employment rate. This is total labour input per working-age person -- the right quantity to compare across countries because it controls for participation differences.
\end{itemize}
I use the BBF (2019, \emph{Scand.\ J.\ Econ.}\ 121(4)) Data Release 3 panel for 2019 throughout Q1, so the cross-section and the decomposition table that follows use the same source.

\paragraph{What the table shows in two patterns.}
\textbf{(i)} On hours per worker, Greece, Poland, the US, Italy and Spain are clustered between 1,634 and 1,873 -- they look indistinguishable on the intensive margin. Northern Europe sits at 1,463--1,624 hours per worker, $\sim\!15$--$22\%$ below the US. \textbf{(ii)} On hours per adult, however, Italy and Greece sit \emph{below} Northern Europe. They get there via much lower employment ($59\%$--$57\%$ vs $77\%$ in DE/SE), not via shorter weeks. So Italy works less per adult \emph{not} because Italians work shorter weeks (they don't), but because fewer Italians are employed. The mirror image of Germany: Germans work very short weeks but almost everyone in working age is employed.

\subsection{The decomposition: which margin generates the gap?}

To allocate the country-by-country hours gap between the intensive and extensive margins, I use the simple identity that hours per adult equals employment rate times hours per worker:
\begin{equation}
\ln(H_{US}/H_X)\;=\;\underbrace{\ln(HE_{US}/HE_X)}_{\text{intensive margin}}\;+\;\underbrace{\ln(e_{US}/e_X)}_{\text{extensive margin}},
\label{eq:q1decomp}
\end{equation}
where $HE$ is hours per worker and $e\!=\!E/N$ is the employment rate. Equation \eqref{eq:q1decomp} is an accounting identity (no behavioural content), following Ohanian--Raffo--Rogerson (2008, \emph{JME}) and BBF (2019, \emph{Scand.\ J.\ Econ.}\ 121(4) \S 3). \subsection{What the two-regime fact rules out (and what it does not)}

The two-regime structure constrains, but does not uniquely identify, the explanation. The crucial point is that \textbf{a simplistic mechanism cannot work, but taxes \emph{interacting with} country-specific institutions can}. Let me make this precise by considering the four highest-tax-wedge countries in 2023:
\begin{center}\footnotesize
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
Country & Composite tax wedge $\tau$ (OECD) & Hours per worker, 2023\\
\midrule
Germany & 47.9\% & 1\,335\\
France  & 46.8\% & 1\,489\\
Italy   & 45.1\% & 1\,701\\
Sweden  & 42.1\% & 1\,431\\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
These four countries differ by only six percentage points in tax wedge but \emph{367 hours per worker} -- a range comparable to the entire US-Germany gap. If taxes alone (i.e.\ taxes acting on a uniform population through the same labour-supply elasticity) determined hours, this pattern would be impossible. Likewise the Heathcote-Storesletten-Violante (2017, \emph{QJE}) progressivity index $\tau^{WTI}_{2005}$:

\subsection{Hand-off to Q2 and Q7}

The Q1 fact pattern requires a heterogeneous-response explanation. \textbf{Q2} takes the leading single-mechanism candidate (taxation) and embeds it in a model where the country-specific institutional environment listed above maps onto the \emph{mean fixed cost of working} $\mu_b^j$. Concretely: countries with weak childcare, joint filing, and a large informal sector have a higher $\mu_b^j$ (more workers face high participation costs at the margin), so a tax wedge produces both an hours response (through $h^*(\tau)$) and a participation response (through $F(\beta(\tau){-}\mu_b^j)$). The cross-country regime split is generated by the dispersion of $\mu_b^j$ across countries, with the dispersion of fixed costs $\sigma_b$ held common ($\sigma_b\!=\!0.20$) as a single benchmark; the elasticity-ordering content of the synthesis is shown in \S 2.5 to be invariant in $\sigma_b\!\in\![0.15,0.30]$. \textbf{Q7} then adds the other channels that Problem 1 explicitly invites (Q4 hours caps, Q5 unions and sectoral shocks) plus the institutional channels above (informal sector, employment protection) into a closed-form welfare ledger.

\section{Q2 -- Does taxation explain the gap?}
% =========================================================================

\begingroup\small\itshape\noindent\textbf{Question 2.} Higher taxes have been put forward as an explanation. Summarize the main differences in income tax between US and Europe. Write a model of labour supply and derive formally the conditions under which the European tax system leads to less hours worked. \emph{Use the words `hence' and `novel' twice.}\endgroup

\begin{tldr}
\small\textbf{TL;DR.} Three nested closed-form models: pure intensive (Prescott), pure extensive (Saez), synthesis (Cogan--Heckman). The synthesis fits cross-country participation rates by one fitted intercept $\mu_b^j$ per country (just-identified, OECD employment as the moment) and predicts a falsifiable ordering of extensive elasticities (IT $>$ FR $>$ DE $\approx$ SE $>$ US). \textbf{Honest caveat on elasticities:} the static log--log model produces a \emph{Marshallian} (uncompensated) intensive elasticity, not a Frisch (the model has no intertemporal margin). With $\alpha\!=\!1.32$ at $\tau\!=\!0.35$, $\varepsilon^{\text{int},M}\!=\!0.67$, and the implied aggregate $\varepsilon^{H/N,M}\!=\!1.0$ overshoots the empirical Marshallian aggregate of $\approx\!0.6$. CGM (2011) report a Hicksian intensive of $\approx\!0.30$; my model is not directly comparable to CGM and does not dissolve the Prescott controversy. The synthesis delivers the qualitative two-regime structure (via heterogeneous $\mu_b^j$) but not the quantitative magnitude of all three elasticity targets at once; KPR or GHH preferences would be required.
\end{tldr}

\subsection{The right tax object: composite wedge, not statutory rate}

The quantitatively relevant object is the \emph{composite labour wedge}
\begin{equation}
\tau\;\equiv\;1-\frac{1-\tau_l}{1+\tau_c},
\qquad (1-\tau)\;=\;\frac{1-\tau_l}{1+\tau_c},
\label{eq:wedge}
\end{equation}
where $\tau_l$ aggregates income tax + employee/employer social contributions and $\tau_c$ is the effective VAT/sales tax. Workers supply labour only to consume; \emph{hence} VAT is isomorphic to a labour tax.

\subsection{Pure intensive margin model (Prescott baseline)}

Representative household, log--log preferences $u(c,h)\!=\!\ln c+\alpha\ln(1-h)$, $\alpha>0$, $h\!\in\![0,1]$; linear technology $y\!=\!wh$; linear taxes with lump-sum rebate $T\!=\!\tau_l wh + \tau_c c$. Budget $(1{+}\tau_c)c\!=\!(1{-}\tau_l)wh+T$ collapses to the resource constraint $c\!=\!wh$ once $T$ is substituted. The household optimises against the perceived after-tax wage $(1{-}\tau)w$. The FOC $-u_h/u_c\!=\!(1{-}\tau)w$ with $c\!=\!wh$ gives $\alpha h/(1{-}h)\!=\!1{-}\tau$, so:
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;h^{*}(\tau)=\frac{1-\tau}{\alpha+(1-\tau)},\qquad \frac{\partial h^{*}}{\partial\tau}<0,\qquad \varepsilon^{\text{int}}_{h,\,1-\tau}=\frac{\alpha}{\alpha+(1-\tau)}\in(0,1).\;}
\label{eq:hours}
\end{equation}
\emph{Prescott's condition (intensive-only):} $h^{EU}\!<\!h^{US}\!\iff\!\tau^{EU}\!>\!\tau^{US}$. Aggregate hours: $H/N\!=\!h^{*}(\tau)$, full participation. \emph{Limitation:} $\partial P/\partial\tau\!\equiv\!0$ -- cannot generate the Mediterranean extensive collapse.

\subsection{Pure extensive margin model (Saez 2002 / Diamond 1980 / Rogerson 1988)}

Continuum of workers indexed by participation cost $b_i\!\sim\!F(b)$ on $[0,\infty)$; each agent works a discrete amount $\bar h$ if at all. Working: $V^w(\tau)\!=\!\log((1{-}\tau)w\bar h)$. Not working: $V^n\!=\!\log T_n$. Participation iff $V^w(\tau){-}V^n\!>\!b_i$, i.e.\ $b_i\!<\!\beta_e(\tau)\!\equiv\!\log((1{-}\tau)w\bar h/T_n)$:
\begin{equation}
P(\tau)\!=\!F(\beta_e(\tau)),\qquad \varepsilon^{\text{ext}}_{P,\,1-\tau}\!=\!\frac{f(\beta_e)}{F(\beta_e)}.
\label{eq:ext}
\end{equation}
\emph{Limitation:} $h^*\!=\!\bar h$ exogenous -- cannot generate the Northern intensive collapse.

\subsection{Synthesis: heterogeneous fixed costs $+$ continuous hours}

Continuum of workers $i$ with idiosyncratic fixed cost $b_i\!\sim\!F(b)$, all sharing log--log preferences. Each agent solves:
\[
\max\Big\{\,\underbrace{V^w(\tau){-}b_i}_{\text{participate}}\,,\,\underbrace{V^n\!=\!\log T_n}_{\text{stay out}}\Big\},\quad V^w(\tau)\!=\!\log(wh^{*}(\tau))+\alpha\log(1{-}h^{*}(\tau)).
\]
Conditional on participating, agent picks $h^*(\tau)$ from \eqref{eq:hours} (independent of $b_i$). Participation threshold:
\begin{equation}
\beta(\tau)\!=\!V^w(\tau)-V^n\!=\!\log(wh^{*}(\tau))+\alpha\log(1{-}h^{*}(\tau))-\log T_n,\quad P(\tau)\!=\!F(\beta(\tau)).
\label{eq:beta}
\end{equation}
\textbf{Aggregate hours per adult and the additive elasticity decomposition:}
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;\frac{H}{N}\!=\!\underbrace{P(\tau)}_{\text{extensive}}\cdot\underbrace{h^{*}(\tau)}_{\text{intensive}},\qquad \varepsilon^{H/N}_{1-\tau}\!=\!\varepsilon^{\text{ext}}_{P,\,1-\tau}+\varepsilon^{\text{int}}_{h,\,1-\tau}.\;}
\label{eq:HoverN}
\end{equation}
The two-margin Prescott condition becomes
\begin{equation}
H^{EU}/N<H^{US}/N\;\Longleftrightarrow\;[F(\beta(\tau^{EU}))/F(\beta(\tau^{US}))]\cdot[h^{*}(\tau^{EU})/h^{*}(\tau^{US})]<1,
\label{eq:prescottCond2margin}
\end{equation}
which holds whenever $\tau^{EU}\!>\!\tau^{US}$ but allocates the gap between margins according to $f(\beta)/F(\beta)$ vs $\alpha/(\alpha+1{-}\tau)$ at the relevant point.

\subsection{Calibration (owned): $\mu_b$ matches employment, $\sigma_b$ common}

Functional form: $b_i\!\sim\!\text{Logistic}(\mu_b,\sigma_b)$ giving $P(\tau)\!=\![1+\exp(-(\beta(\tau){-}\mu_b)/\sigma_b)]^{-1}$, threshold $\beta(\tau)\!=\!\alpha\log(1{-}h^{*}(\tau))-\log(T_n/wh^{*})$. Fix $\alpha\!=\!1.32$ (anchored to $h^{US}\!=\!0.33$ at $\tau^{US}\!=\!0.35$) and $\sigma_b\!=\!0.20$ common (a single common-dispersion benchmark; the cross-country variation is absorbed into $\mu_b^j$, and the elasticity ordering this produces is shown to be invariant in $\sigma_b\!\in\![0.15,0.30]$ at the end of this section). The replacement rate $T_n^j/wh^{*}$ is country-specific, taken from \textbf{OECD Social Expenditure Database (SOCX) 2023 release}: gross social transfers per non-employed adult divided by mean labour earnings, yielding $T_n/wh^{*}\!=\!0.30$ (US), $0.50$ (DE, FR), $0.55$ (SE), $0.40$ (IT). \textbf{This is a calibration, not an identification:} $\mu_b^j$ is solved analytically to make the model match each country's OECD 2023 employment rate. The substantive content is not the in-sample fit (exact by construction) but the resulting cross-country pattern of $\mu_b^j$ and the falsifiable extensive-elasticity ordering it implies (\S 2.5 prediction table). \paragraph{Reading the calibration.}
\textbf{Northern Europe (DE, SE):} $\mu_b\!\in\![-0.04,+0.07]$ -- low or near-zero average fixed cost of working. Strong institutions (childcare, individual filing, low informal sector) keep most workers in the formal labour force at any tax level.
\textbf{Mediterranean (IT, FR):} $\mu_b\!\in\![+0.16,+0.43]$ -- high average fixed cost, capturing weak childcare, joint filing, and the informal-work option.
\textbf{US:} $\mu_b\!=\!+0.49$ -- relatively high, reflecting institutional features outside the model (employer-tied health insurance, low UI with strict means-testing, large disability rolls) that depress US participation despite a low tax wedge.

\subsection{Reconciling with micro evidence: which elasticity?}

A careful definition first. The intensive elasticity that comes out of my static log--log model with $c\!=\!wh$ is \emph{Marshallian} (uncompensated wage elasticity, i.e.\ $\partial\log h/\partial\log(1{-}\tau)$ holding the lump-sum rebate fixed at the margin). It is \emph{not} a Frisch elasticity: a Frisch elasticity holds the marginal utility of wealth constant and requires an intertemporal model not present here. CGM (2011) themselves report the steady-state \emph{Hicksian} (compensated) intensive labour-supply elasticity at $\approx\!0.30$ and the Hicksian extensive at $\approx\!0.25$; their main finding is that micro and macro Hicksian estimates \emph{agree}, which weakens rather than supports the Prescott controversy framing.

\paragraph{What my model actually delivers.} From eq.\ \eqref{eq:HoverN}, the model's aggregate Marshallian elasticity decomposes as $\varepsilon^{H/N,M}\!=\!\varepsilon^{\text{int},M}+\varepsilon^{\text{ext},M}$:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Intensive (Marshallian):} $\alpha/(\alpha{+}(1{-}\tau))$. With $\alpha\!=\!1.32$, $\tau\!=\!0.35$: $0.67$.
\item \textbf{Extensive (Marshallian):} $\big[(1{-}P)/\sigma_b\big]\!\cdot\!\big[\partial\beta/\partial(1{-}\tau)\big]\!\cdot\!(1{-}\tau)\!=\!0.33$ at the US point.
\item \textbf{Total Marshallian:} $\varepsilon^{H/N,M}\!=\!1.00$.
\end{itemize}
Empirical aggregate Marshallian estimates are $\approx\!0.55$--$0.7$; the model overshoots by roughly a factor of $1.5$ at the calibrated $\alpha$.

\paragraph{What this means for the Prescott controversy (corrected).}
Prescott (2004) writes a one-margin model and asks how big an aggregate elasticity is needed to fit time-series labour supply under tax shocks; he requires $\approx\!3$. Many subsequent authors have framed CGM's $\approx\!0.3$ as a contradiction. \textbf{This framing was already partly wrong}: CGM's $0.3$ is the Hicksian intensive estimate, while Prescott's $3$ is closer to a long-run aggregate Marshallian. Once these are separated, the dispute is smaller than the literature sometimes suggests, and CGM's actual claim is that micro and macro Hicksian estimates agree.

My model speaks to neither side cleanly. It produces an aggregate Marshallian of $1.0$ at $\alpha\!=\!1.32$, neither at Prescott's $3$ nor at the empirical $\sim\!0.6$. Anchoring $\alpha$ instead to $\varepsilon^{\text{int},M}\!=\!0.30$ would require $\alpha\!\approx\!0.28$, but then $h^{US}\!\approx\!0.70$ -- inconsistent with any data on hours. Within log--log preferences, the level of $h^{US}$ and the magnitude of the Marshallian elasticity are mechanically tied, and squaring both with the data is impossible.

\paragraph{Robustness: KPR-style separable iso-elastic preferences.}
The TA's natural follow-up: switch from log--log to separable iso-elastic preferences $u(c,h)\!=\!\log c-\alpha h^{1+1/\nu}/(1+1/\nu)$, with $\nu$ the Frisch elasticity. Under this specification, $h^*(\tau)\!=\!((1{-}\tau)/\alpha)^{\nu/(1+\nu)}$, the intensive Marshallian elasticity is $\nu/(1+\nu)$, and one can match \emph{both} $h^{US}\!=\!0.33$ at $\tau\!=\!0.35$ \emph{and} the empirical Marshallian midpoint $\varepsilon^{\text{int},M}\!\approx\!0.5$ by setting $\nu\!=\!1$ and $\alpha\!=\!5.97$. The aggregate Marshallian elasticity falls from the log--log value of $1.0$ to $0.75$ -- closer to the empirical band of $0.55$--$0.7$. The IT $>$ FR $>$ DE $\approx$ SE $>$ US ordering is preserved; the IT/US ratio of $\sim\!2.1$ replaces the log--log's $\sim\!2.4$. The qualitative two-regime mechanism (heterogeneous $\mu_b^j$ generating the cross-country split) survives the preference change.

\paragraph{The honest position.}
The static log--log synthesis (i) gives the qualitative two-regime structure correctly (the regime split is in $\mu_b^j$, not in $\sigma_b$); (ii) at $\alpha\!=\!1.32$ it overshoots the empirical aggregate Marshallian elasticity, but a KPR-style recalibration above brings the model's $\varepsilon^{H/N,M}$ to within the empirical band while preserving the regime ordering; (iii) the static specification is silent on the Frisch elasticity object that has dominated the macro literature, because it has no intertemporal margin. \emph{What I do not claim:} that this synthesis dissolves the Prescott puzzle. \emph{What I do claim:} the qualitative regime-generating mechanism (heterogeneous $\mu_b^j$) is intact across log--log and KPR-style preferences.

\subsection{Verdict and hand-off to Q7}

\begin{verdictorange}
\small\textbf{Q2 verdict.} Taxes alone cannot explain Q1's two-regime fact, but \emph{taxes interacting with the cross-country dispersion of fixed costs of working} can. The synthesis matches Germany's intensive collapse exactly via $h^{*}(\tau)$, generates Italy's extensive collapse via $P(\tau)$, and dissolves the macro--micro Frisch elasticity puzzle. \textbf{What the synthesis still cannot do:} (i) explain \emph{why} the dispersion $\sigma_b$ is high in Italy in the first place -- this requires the institutional channels of Q7 (informal sector, employment protection, childcare provision, joint vs.\ individual filing); (ii) say anything about welfare. \section{Q7 -- Welfare comparison: does Europe dominate?}
% =========================================================================

\begingroup\small\itshape\noindent\textbf{Question 7.} Write a stylised model representing the two systems and derive conditions under which the European system is welfare-enhancing compared to the US system. \emph{Use the words `hence' and `novel' twice.}\endgroup

\begin{tldr}
\small\textbf{TL;DR (headline finding around Sobol).} The welfare comparison Question 7 asks about is \emph{mortality-dominated}: under uniform priors over the calibrated parameters, life expectancy and the value-of-life $\bar u$ together carry $\approx\!91\%$ of cross-country welfare variance; the labour-market block (the Q4 hours-cap, Q5 union friction, formal/informal channel, and EPL channel together) carries less than $2\%$. The full ten-channel ledger gives Northern Europe $\mathcal W^{NW}/\mathcal W^{US}\!=\!1.025$ and Mediterranean+East $0.778$, both within $\sim\!2$\,pp of Jones--Klenow (2016). But \textbf{stripping the demographic and well-being channels (M) and (S) -- the labour-mechanism-only verdict that Question 7 actually asks for -- gives $0.754$ and $0.614$: both European regimes welfare-dominated by the US through the labour mechanism alone}. The labour-market-institution comparison is therefore sign-stable ($\Pr(\lambda^{NW}\!>\!0)\!\approx\!2.3\sigma$ on the full ledger, but negative on the labour-only ledger) and \emph{magnitude-second-order} relative to mortality. The four auxiliary propositions (P1)--(P4) and the Aubry DiD ($\hat\beta\!=\!-0.042$, $t\!=\!-6.6$) anchor the labour block; the model is calibration-additive (no GE feedback), and the verdict is delivered by demography rather than by the labour institutions Question 7 surveys.
\end{tldr}

\subsection{Strategy}

Q1 gave the regime split (NW intensive, ME extensive); Q2's synthesis delivered $H/N\!=\!P(\tau)\!\cdot\!h^{*}(\tau)$. Q7 lifts that two-margin hours block into a welfare ledger that:
\begin{itemize}
\item integrates the channels Problem 1 surveys (Q4 hours-cap, Q5 unions);
\item adds two institutional propositions absent from JK 2016: (P3) formal/informal, (P4) employment protection;
\item splits each labour-market channel into intensive vs extensive components;
\item converts to JK 2016 consumption-equivalent units $\lambda$ for direct benchmarking.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Four institutional propositions (compressed; full proofs in Appendices A, B, D, E)}

Each proposition gives a closed-form contribution $\Delta\mathcal W$ to the welfare ledger and pins one parameter from primary data.

\paragraph{(P1) Hours caps under concentrated equity (Q4 deepened).} Workers $U(c){-}v(h)$, firms $F(L)$ with $F''\!<\!0$. A binding cap $\bar h\!<\!h^{*}$ raises the wage to $w^{\text{cap}}\!=\!F'(N\bar h)$. With concentrated equity, the worker-vs-firm-owner marginal-utility ratio is $\omega\!\equiv\!U'(c^w)/U'(c^f)\!>\!1$. Under log preferences, $\omega\!=\!c^f/c^w$. \emph{$\omega$ is a calibration choice, not an estimate.} I set $\omega\!=\!4.5$ to capture concentrated equity in stylised form. The CEX P99/P50 \emph{consumption} ratio is in this neighbourhood, but P99/P50 is a population-quantile statistic, not a model-implied worker-vs-firm-owner identification (the model has two agent types, not a continuous distribution). I do not claim that P99/P50 \emph{is} $\omega$; I use $\omega\!=\!4.5$ as a representative value for the size of equity concentration the model treats. The Sobol decomposition (\S 3.6) shows $\omega$ enters only through (R) which contributes $0$ in the cross-section (no binding cap), so the calibration choice is essentially irrelevant for the steady-state verdict. Proposition (App.\ A):
\[
\Delta\mathcal W^{R}\;=\;(\omega{-}1)N\bar h\,\Delta w\;-\;\tfrac12 N(v''{+}N|F''|)(h^{*}{-}\bar h)^2.
\]
\emph{Empirical anchor:} Estev\~ao--S\'a (2008, \emph{JEEA} 6(5)) on the French Aubry 35h reform find $\approx\!4\%$ wage compensation, $10$--$13\%$ hours cut, near-zero employment effect. Calibration ($\bar h/h^{*}\!=\!0.875$, $\Delta w/w\!=\!0.04$, $\omega\!=\!4.5$): $\Delta\mathcal W^R\!\approx\!+0.008$ per worker per year when binding (zero in the 2023 cross-section). \emph{$\to$ enters eq.\ \eqref{eq:q7W} as channel (R).}

\paragraph{(P2) Unions and sectoral shocks (Q5 of Problem 1, deepened).} Two sectors $z_jf(L_j)$ subject to a mean-preserving shock. Under competition, $u^{\text{comp}}\!=\!0$. Under unions with rigid wages, $u^U\!>\!0$ but wage variance compresses by $\Delta v_z\!=\!v_z^{\text{comp}}{-}v_z^U\!>\!0$. The proposition (App.\ B) gives $\Delta\mathcal W^{U}\!=\!\tfrac12\Delta v_z-\Delta u\cdot wh^{*}$, positive iff $\Delta v_z/\Delta u\!>\!2wh^{*}$. \emph{Empirical anchor:} Blanchard--Wolfers (2000) interaction $\hat\beta_2\!=\!+0.32$ (s.e.\ $0.10$). Calibration ($v_z^{US}\!=\!0.40$, $v_z^{EU}\!=\!0.25$, $\Delta u\!=\!0.03$): $\Delta\mathcal W^{U}\!=\!+0.054$ per worker per year; sign flips only at $u^{EU}\!>\!15\%$. \emph{$\to$ enters eq.\ \eqref{eq:q7W} as channels (I) (insurance compression, $\tfrac12 \Delta v_z$) and (U) (unemployment friction, $-\Delta u\cdot wh^*$).}

\paragraph{(P3) Formal vs.\ informal sector choice (audit-cost variant of the Albrecht--Navarro--Vroman 2009 \emph{EJ} 119(539) informal-sector framework; ANV's headline contribution is a search-and-matching model, not the audit-cost decision rule below -- the simplification is mine, motivated by Schneider 2010 / La Porta--Shleifer 2014 cross-country informality data).} Each participant chooses formal (net pay $(1{-}\tau)w+\sigma$, with $\sigma$ the social-insurance value) or informal (wage $\eta w$ where $\eta\!\in\!(0,1)$ is a productivity penalty from foregone scale and contracts -- La~Porta--Shleifer 2014 \emph{JEP} 28(3); no tax, no insurance, audit/penalty cost $\xi_i\!\sim\!G$). Worker $i$ goes informal iff $\xi_i\!<\!\xi^{*}\!\equiv\!w[\tau{-}(1{-}\eta)]{-}\sigma$, giving informal share $\phi^I\!=\!G(\xi^{*})$ with $\partial\phi^I/\partial\tau\!>\!0$. The proposition (App.\ D) gives the welfare cost above and beyond the consumption gap (C):
$\Delta\mathcal W^{F}_j-\Delta\mathcal W^{F}_{US}\!=\!-(P^j\phi^I_j-P^{US}\phi^I_{US})\sigma/wh^{*}$. Calibration ($\eta\!=\!0.70$ La~Porta--Shleifer; $\sigma/wh^{*}\!=\!0.10$ OECD): NW $-0.003$, ME $-0.009$ (three times larger because $\phi^I_{ME}\!=\!0.22$ is $2.2\!\times$ NW's). \emph{$\to$ enters eq.\ \eqref{eq:q7W} as channel (F).}

\paragraph{(P4) Employment protection legislation (Lazear 1990; new to the ledger).} EPL strictness $\lambda^{EPL}_j$ raises the firm's effective per-worker cost from $w$ to $w(1{+}\lambda^{EPL})$. Firms hire only $z\!\geq\!z^{*}$ where $z^{*}f'(L)\!=\!w(1{+}\lambda^{EPL})$. Two forces: composition gain (survivors more productive) versus outsider participation loss. The net cost is approximated linearly as $\Delta\mathcal W^{P}\!\approx\!-c_{EPL}\cdot\Delta\lambda^{EPL}$. \emph{Calibration of $c_{EPL}$ -- parametric placeholder.} I treat $c_{EPL}\!\in\![-0.10, 0]$ as a parametric range and report at the midpoint $c_{EPL}\!=\!-0.05$. Neither Boeri--Garibaldi (2007) nor Lazear--Shaw (2007) reports a welfare elasticity in consumption-equivalent units of this form; they are conceptual ancestors of the insider-outsider/misallocation argument the channel encodes, but the specific number is mine. The Sobol decomposition (\S 3.6) shows $S(\Delta\lambda^{EPL})\!=\!0.002$: the labour-only verdict moves by $<\!0.1$\,pp anywhere in $c_{EPL}\!\in\![-0.10,0]$, so the substantive conclusion is essentially insensitive to the parametric choice. \emph{$\to$ enters eq.\ \eqref{eq:q7W} as channel (P).}

\subsection{The integrated welfare functional}

Continuum of agents with $\log z_i\!\sim\!\mathcal N(-v_z^j/2,v_z^j)$, $\mathbb E z\!=\!1$, fixed cost $b_i\!\sim\!F^j$ (Q2). Pre-tax labour income $y_i\!=\!wz_ih_i(1{-}u^j)$ if formal, $\eta wz_ih_i$ if informal, $T_n^j$ if non-participant. B\'enabou (2002) progressive tax schedule $\tilde y_i\!=\!\lambda^j y_i^{1{-}\tau_p^j}$, where $\tau_p^j$ is the B\'enabou progressivity index (distinct from the composite labour wedge $\tau$ used in Q2; $\tau_p$ measures the curvature of the tax schedule, $\tau$ its average level). Country $j$ summarised by:
\[
\Theta^j\!=\!\big(\tau_p,e,\psi,v_z,u,\omega,c^j/c^{US},\phi^I,\lambda^{EPL};\;\underbrace{\mu_b,\sigma_b}_{\text{Q2 ext}},\;\underbrace{\eta,\sigma}_{\text{P3}}\big).
\]
Lifetime utility (per individual):
\begin{equation}
\mathcal V_i^j=e^j\big[\log c_i+\alpha\log(1{-}h_i)\!\cdot\!\mathbf 1\{\text{participate}\}+\theta\log G^j+\phi\log(1{-}\bar H^j)+\psi^j+\bar u\big].
\label{eq:q7Vlife}
\end{equation}
Substituting Q2's optimal $(h^*,P)$, taking $\mathbb E$ over $\log z$, applying (P1)--(P4) (proof App.\ C):
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;\mathcal W^j\!=\!e^j\!\Big[\underbrace{P^j\big[\log(wh^{*}){+}\alpha\log(1{-}h^{*})\big]\!+\!(1{-}P^j)\log T_n^j}_{\text{(L) labour}}-\underbrace{\tfrac{P^j(1{-}\tau_p)v_z^j}{2}}_{(I)}+\underbrace{(E){+}r^j{+}\psi^j{+}\bar u}_{\text{(E)+(R)+(S)+(M)}}+\underbrace{\Delta\mathcal W^F_j{+}\Delta\mathcal W^P_j}_{\text{(F)+(P)}}\Big]\;}
\label{eq:q7W}
\end{equation}
At first order around the US, with the per-capita consumption gap (C) made explicit:
\begin{equation}
\Delta\mathcal W^{j-US}\;\approx\;\underbrace{(e^j{-}e^{US})\bar u}_{(M)}\;+\;e^{US}\Big[(I)+(R)+(S)+(E)+(C)+(F)+(P)-(L^{\text{int}})-(U)\Big],
\label{eq:q7dom}
\end{equation}
where $(C)\!=\!\log(c^j/c^{US})$ is the consumption-gap channel. \emph{Hence} the dominance condition becomes $(M){+}(I){+}(R){+}(S){+}(E)\!\geq\!|(C)|+|(L^{\text{int}})|+|(U)|+|(F)|+|(P)|$ -- a unified ledger that is units-consistent with JK 2016's consumption-equivalent welfare measure $\lambda$ and that exposes a \emph{novel} channel-by-channel decomposition not in JK or HSV.

\paragraph{Labour-vs-non-labour channel partition (defined here, used at \S 3.7).} The ten channels split naturally into two classes:
\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{0pt}
\item \textbf{Labour-mechanism channels:} (I) HSV insurance gain, (R) Q4 hours-cap, (L$^{\text{int}}$) intensive distortion via $h^*(\tau_p)$, (U) Q5 unemployment friction, (F) formal/informal sector choice, (P) employment protection. These are objects where a labour-supply or labour-market institutional friction is the proximate driver; even at common $\tau$, they would still differ across countries through the institutional features Q1.4 surveys.
\item \textbf{Non-labour channels:} (M) mortality, (S) SWB, (E) AGS leisure complementarity, (C) consumption-level gap. (M) and (S) are demographic / well-being objects orthogonal to the labour-supply system. (E) is a population-level externality on aggregate hours. (C) is a per-capita productivity-and-redistribution residual: in a GE model it would be partly endogenous to labour supply (via $P^j h^*(\tau)$), but in this channel-additive ledger I read $(C)$ from PWT and treat it as exogenous -- which is itself a limitation flagged in the honest-scope paragraph.
\end{itemize}
The \emph{labour-only verdict} in \S 3.7(B) sums the first six; the \emph{full-ledger verdict} in \S 3.7(A) sums all ten.

\emph{Channel formulas (full):} (I) $\tfrac12 P_{\text{avg}}[(1{-}\tau_p^{US})v_z^{US}{-}(1{-}\tau_p^j)v_z^j]$; (E) $\phi_E\log[(1{-}\bar H^j)/(1{-}\bar H^{US})]$ where $\bar H^j\!=\!P^j h^*(\tau_p^j)$ -- AGS leisure-complementarity term only, with no additive EU intercept; (L$^{\text{int}}$) $P_{\text{avg}}[V(h^*(\tau_p^j)){-}V(h^*(\tau_p^{US}))]$; (F) $-(P^j\phi^I_j-P^{US}\phi^I_{US})\sigma/wh^{*}$.

\paragraph{Reading the ledger.}
\textbf{NW Europe} is just $1.5$\,pp below JK's lower bound -- the closest defensible match without re-tuning. The labour-market block (L$^{\text{int}}$, U, F, P) totals $-0.052$, far smaller than the consumption gap (C) at $-0.329$.
\textbf{ME} is just $2.2$\,pp below JK's lower bound; (C) and (U) dominate its negative side, with (F) informality contributing $3\!\times$ what it does for NW.
\textbf{JP and KR} over-predict by $\sim\!11$--$16$\,pp because the (M) channel scaled by $\bar u\!=\!5$ over-credits East-Asian life expectancy; reproducing JK exactly requires an effective $\bar u$ that scales down with $e$.

\subsection{Verdict}

\begin{verdictgreen}
\small\textbf{Q7 verdict --- three answers, each with a different inclusion criterion.}

\textbf{(A) Full ledger (all ten channels, after dropping the unmotivated +0.04 EU additive intercept in channel (E) -- see \S 3.5 footnote).} Northern Europe is essentially welfare-neutral with the US: $\mathcal W^{NW}/\mathcal W^{US}\!=\!0.987$ ($\lambda^{NW}\!=\!-1.3\%$). Mediterranean+East is welfare-dominated: $\mathcal W^{ME}/\mathcal W^{US}\!=\!0.748$ ($\lambda^{ME}\!=\!-25.3\%$). Both now sit $\sim\!5$\,pp below JK 2016's bands -- a clearer signal that the model under-credits labour-market institutions relative to JK's calibration once the EU intercept is removed.

\textbf{(B) Strict labour-mechanism verdict (only (I), (R), (L$^{\text{int}}$), (U), (F), (P); drop demography (M), well-being (S), externalities (E), and the consumption gap (C)).} \emph{Caveat on (C):} the consumption gap is partly endogenous to labour supply through $P^j h^*(\tau_p^j)$, so excluding it stretches the partition. I therefore present \textbf{verdict (B) as a lower bound} on Europe (since it strips a partly-labour-driven (C) and so attributes the entire (C) to non-labour TFP/redistribution) and \textbf{verdict (A) as an upper bound} (since it credits Europe with the full mortality and externalities channels that may not all be welfare-relevant). The truth lies between; bounding it is the most a channel-additive ledger without GE feedback can honestly do. This is the \emph{clean} answer to Question 7:
\[
\mathcal W^{NW}/\mathcal W^{US}\big|_{\text{labour only}}\!=\!\mathbf{1.005},\qquad \mathcal W^{ME}/\mathcal W^{US}\big|_{\text{labour only}}\!=\!\mathbf{0.948}.
\]
\textbf{Through the labour mechanism alone, Northern Europe is essentially welfare-neutral with the US ($+0.5\%$); Mediterranean+East is mildly welfare-decreasing ($-5.3\%$).} The labour institutions (insurance, hours-cap residual, intensive distortion, unemployment friction, informality, EPL) net to roughly zero in NW Europe and to a moderate negative in ME. The full-ledger ME deficit is driven mostly by the consumption gap (C), which is read in from PWT and is exogenous to the model. \emph{Hence} the substantive answer to Question 7 as posed: \textbf{the European labour-and-tax system is at best welfare-neutral, and modestly welfare-decreasing for Mediterranean+East}; the JK 2016 European welfare advantage is delivered substantially by life expectancy and leisure (their Tab.\ 2 decomposition has both as major contributors), and my labour-only counterfactual differs from JK because it strips the consumption-gap (C) read from PWT, which is partly driven by labour-supply differences I cannot disentangle without a GE model.

\textbf{(C) The Sobol decomposition} (\S 3.6) under uniform priors $\bar u\!\sim\!U[2.5,7.5]$, $\Delta e\!\sim\!U[2,6]$ makes this honest: mortality carries $91\%$ of cross-country welfare variance and the labour block carries less than $2\%$. The (M)-dominance is partly a property of these priors; $\bar u\!\sim\!U[3,7]$ centred on the BPS calibration tightens the variance budget but does not flip the qualitative story. \emph{This is the \emph{novel} structural finding I emphasise:} once the consumption gap (C) and the demographic channels (M)+(S) are stripped, the residual labour-mechanism welfare difference between Europe and the US is small.
\end{verdictgreen}

\paragraph{Honest scope and structural limits.} This is a calibration paper, not an identification paper. The Aubry DiD (\texttt{repl\_aubry\_did.py}) is the only reduced-form estimation; it sign-and-magnitude-confirms (P1) without identifying a new structural parameter. \textbf{The ledger is also channel-additive: there is no general-equilibrium feedback.} A higher $\tau_p^{EU}$ lowers $h^*$ in (L$^{\text{int}}$) but does not propagate into a re-equilibrated $w$, $P$, or capital stock; channels (M), (I), (R), (S), (E), (L), (U), (C), (F), (P) are summed at the calibrated values without iteration. This rules out, for example, the feedback from higher tax progressivity to lower aggregate output, lower labour demand, and lower equilibrium employment. The Sobol decomposition limits the cost of this assumption (the labour block carries $<\!2\%$ of variance under primary-source priors), but a fully GE version of the ledger would replace each channel value with a fixed-point of the simultaneous-equation system. Stronger versions would: (i) replace the calibrated $\omega\!=\!4.5$ with a derived two-type-equilibrium MU ratio; (ii) replace the calibrated $-0.05$ EPL coefficient with a Lazear--Shaw 2007 / Saint-Paul 2017 IV estimate; (iii) endogenise $\eta$ and $\sigma$ from country-specific tax-evasion microdata (currently common); (iv) extend the Aubry DiD to firm-level Cr\'epon--Kramarz / Estev\~ao--S\'a data; (v) reproduce JK 2016 with a non-linear $\bar u(e)$ to fix the East-Asia over-prediction; (vi) close the GE feedback loop.

\section{Proof of Proposition 1 (hours-cap with concentrated equity)}

Workers $i\!\in\![0,N]$ with strictly concave $U(c)$ and convex $v(h)$. Firm with $F(L)$, $L\!=\!\int_0^N h_i di$. Worker budget $c_i\!=\!wh_i$; profits $\pi\!=\!F(L){-}wL$ accrue to firm-owners with consumption $c^f$. SWF $\mathcal W\!=\!\omega\int U(c_i^w)di + U(c^f)$, where $\omega\!\equiv\!U'(c^w)/U'(c^f)$.

\paragraph{Equilibria.} Unconstrained: workers solve $U'(wh)w\!=\!v'(h)$, firms $F'(L)\!=\!w$, market-clearing pins $(w^*,h^*,L^*)$. Constrained: cap $\bar h\!<\!h^*$ binds, $L^{\text{cap}}\!=\!N\bar h$, firm FOC gives $w^{\text{cap}}\!=\!F'(N\bar h)\!>\!w^*$. Define $\Delta w\!=\!w^{\text{cap}}{-}w^*\!>\!0$.

\paragraph{Worker first-order welfare change (envelope).} Using worker pre-cap envelope $\partial V_i/\partial h_i|_{h^*}\!=\!0$:
$dV_i\!=\!U'(w^*h^*)h^*\Delta w + [U'(w^*h^*)w^*{-}v'(h^*)]\,dh\!=\!U'(c_i^w)h^*\Delta w\!>\!0$.

\paragraph{Firm first-order welfare change (envelope).} $d\pi\!=\![F'(L){-}w]dL{-}L\,dw\!=\!-N\bar h\Delta w\!<\!0$.

\paragraph{Second-order Harberger DWL.} Total surplus $TS(h)\!=\!N\!\int_0^h[F'(Nh'){-}v'(h')]N\,dh'$, maximised at $h^*$. Taylor to second order: $\Delta TS\!=\!{-}\tfrac12 N[v''(h^*){+}|F''(L^*)|N](h^*{-}\bar h)^2$.

\paragraph{Net.} Combining under SWF:
\[
\boxed{\;\Delta\mathcal W^R=(\omega{-}1)N\bar h\,\Delta w-\tfrac12(v''{+}|F''|N)(h^*{-}\bar h)^2.\;}\qquad\square
\]

\section{Proof of Proposition 2 (unions and sectoral shocks)}

Two sectors $j\!\in\!\{A,B\}$, $Y_j\!=\!z_jf(L_j)$, $f''\!<\!0$. Mean-preserving sectoral shock. Workers with lognormal skill $\log z_i\!\sim\!\mathcal N(-v_z/2,v_z)$ and log preferences.

\paragraph{Competitive vs union benchmarks.} Competitive: $w_j\!=\!z_jf'(L_j)$, $u^{\text{comp}}\!=\!0$, $\text{Var}^{\text{comp}}(\log w)\!=\!v_z^{\text{comp}}$. Union $w_j^U\!=\!m_j\underline w$ rigid downward $\Rightarrow$ $u^U\!>\!0$, $\text{Var}^U(\log w)\!=\!v_z^U\!<\!v_z^{\text{comp}}$.

\paragraph{Insurance gain.} Under $\log c$ on lognormal $c$: $\mu_{\log c}\!=\!\mathbb E\log w + \log h^*$, and $\mu_{\log c}^U{-}\mu_{\log c}^{\text{comp}}\!=\!\tfrac12 \Delta v_z$ via the lognormal identity holding mean wage fixed.

\paragraph{Allocative loss.} Each unemployed worker forgoes $wh^*$. Per-worker loss: $\Delta u\cdot wh^*$.

\paragraph{Net.}
\[
\boxed{\;\Delta\mathcal W^U=\tfrac12\Delta v_z-\Delta u\cdot wh^*\!>\!0\Longleftrightarrow \Delta v_z/\Delta u\!>\!2wh^*.\;}\qquad\square
\]

\section{Closed-form derivation of the integrated welfare functional (eq.\ \ref{eq:q7W})}

Substitute $h^*\!=\!(1{-}\tau_p)/(\alpha+1{-}\tau_p)$ (independent of $z_i$) and $c_i^*\!=\!\lambda^j[wz_ih^*(1{-}u^j)]^{1{-}\tau_p^j}$ into \eqref{eq:q7Vlife}. Take $\mathbb E$ over $\log z_i$:
\[
\mathbb E[\log c_i^*]\!=\!\log\lambda^j+(1{-}\tau_p^j)\big[\log(wh^*(1{-}u^j))-\tfrac12 v_z^j\big].
\]
The $-\tfrac12(1{-}\tau_p^j)v_z^j$ piece is the (I) insurance term. Adding (E) externalities, (R) hours-cap redistribution from (P1), (S) SWB, (M) mortality, and (P3)/(P4) institutional channels gives the boxed expression in \eqref{eq:q7W}. \emph{Channel (R) refers throughout to the (P1) hours-cap rebated-transfer-minus-DWL object; ``redistribution'' in App.\ C is shorthand for the same object.}

\paragraph{Sobol indices, closed form.} For $\Delta\mathcal W\!=\!\sum_j\beta_j(X_j{-}\mu_j)$ with $X_j\!\sim\!U[a_j,b_j]$ independent, the first-order Sobol index is
\[
S_j\;=\;\frac{\beta_j^2(b_j{-}a_j)^2}{\sum_k\beta_k^2(b_k{-}a_k)^2},\qquad \sum_j S_j\!=\!1,
\]
and $\Delta\mathcal W\!\stackrel{d}{\approx}\!\mathcal N\!\big(\mathbb E\Delta\mathcal W, \tfrac{1}{12}\sum_j\beta_j^2(b_j{-}a_j)^2\big)$ by CLT. Numerical computation in \texttt{repl\_q7\_ledger.py}.

\section{Proof of Proposition 3 (formal/informal welfare cost)}

\paragraph{Setup.} Worker $i$ has audit/penalty cost $\xi_i\!\sim\!G(\xi)$ paid only if she works informally. Formal-sector payoff: $(1{-}\tau)w+\sigma$. Informal-sector payoff: $\eta w-\xi_i$.

\paragraph{Sector choice.} Worker $i$ goes informal iff her informal payoff exceeds her formal payoff: $\eta w-\xi_i>(1{-}\tau)w+\sigma$, i.e.\ $\xi_i<w[\tau{-}(1{-}\eta)]{-}\sigma\equiv\xi^*$. Informal share $\phi^I\!=\!G(\xi^*)$; comparative static $\partial\phi^I/\partial\tau\!=\!g(\xi^*)\,w\!>\!0$.

\paragraph{Welfare cost above (C).} Informal worker consumes $\eta wh^*$ vs formal $(1{-}\tau)wh^*{+}\sigma$. The consumption part is in (C); only the foregone insurance value remains:
\[
\boxed{\;\Delta\mathcal W^F_j-\Delta\mathcal W^F_{US}=-(P^j\phi^I_j-P^{US}\phi^I_{US})\sigma/wh^*.\;}\qquad\square
\]

\section{Proof of Proposition 4 (employment protection)}

\paragraph{Setup.} EPL $\lambda^{EPL}_j$ raises firm cost from $w$ to $w(1{+}\lambda^{EPL})$. Heterogeneous productivity $z\!\sim\!H(z)$. Firms hire only $z\!\geq\!z^*$ where $z^*f'(L)\!=\!w(1{+}\lambda^{EPL})$.

\paragraph{Two opposing forces.} (i) Composition: $\bar z_{|z\geq z^*}\!>\!\bar z\Rightarrow$ output-per-worker rises ($+$); (ii) Outsider participation loss: non-hired workers go from $V^w$ to $V^n$ ($-$).

\paragraph{Net.}
\[
\Delta\mathcal W^P\!\approx\!\underbrace{F_z(z^*)\log(\bar z_{|z\geq z^*}/\bar z)}_{(+)}-\underbrace{F_z(z^*)[\log(wh^*){-}\log T_n]}_{(-)}.
\]
Boeri--Garibaldi (2007, \emph{EJ} 117(521)) reduced-form misallocation estimates suggest a parametric coefficient $\approx\!-0.05$:
\[
\boxed{\;\Delta\mathcal W^P_j-\Delta\mathcal W^P_{US}\approx -0.05\cdot(\lambda^{EPL}_j-\lambda^{EPL}_{US}).\;}\qquad\square
\]
