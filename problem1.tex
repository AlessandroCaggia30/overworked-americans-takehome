\section{Q1 -- Documenting the US--Europe hours gap}
% =========================================================================

\begingroup\small\itshape\noindent\textbf{Question 1.} Document the difference between hours worked in the US vs Europe in recent years, if needed across European countries.\endgroup

\begin{tldr}
\small\textbf{Bottom line.} The standard ``Europe vs US'' framing collapses two distinct facts into one. \emph{Northern Europe} (Germany, Netherlands, Sweden, UK) works less per worker than the US -- the gap is on the \emph{intensive margin}. \emph{Mediterranean+East} (Italy, Spain, Greece, Poland, Hungary) works nearly as many hours per worker as the US but employs a much smaller fraction of the population -- the gap is on the \emph{extensive margin}. The crucial implication for Q2: a tax wedge of $\sim\!55\%$ in Germany and Italy produces a $17\%$ intensive-margin collapse in one country and a $28\%$ extensive-margin collapse in the other. \textbf{No uniform-response story can do this. But taxes \emph{interacting with} country-specific institutions (joint vs.\ individual filing, informal sector, childcare, employment protection) can -- and that is exactly what the Q2 synthesis model captures.}
\end{tldr}

\subsection{The fact: cross-section of hours and employment, 2023 (OECD primary data)}

I begin by laying out the raw cross-country evidence. Three quantities matter, all measured for the working-age population (15--64):
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Hours per worker} = annual hours actually worked by the average employed person. This is the \emph{intensive margin} of labour supply.
\item \textbf{Employment rate $E/N$} = fraction of the working-age population that is employed. This is the \emph{extensive margin}.
\item \textbf{Hours per adult $H/N$} = the product, hours per worker $\times$ employment rate. This is total labour input per working-age person -- the right quantity to compare across countries because it controls for participation differences.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{What the table shows in two patterns.}
\textbf{(i)} On hours per worker, Greece, Poland, the US and Italy are clustered between 1,701 and 1,893 -- they look indistinguishable on the intensive margin. Northern Europe sits at 1,335--1,496 hours per worker, 25--40\% below the US. \textbf{(ii)} On hours per adult, however, Italy and Greece sit \emph{below} Northern Europe. They get there via much lower employment (62\% vs 77\%), not via shorter weeks. So Italy works less per adult \emph{not} because Italians work shorter weeks (they don't), but because fewer Italians are employed. The mirror image of Germany: Germans work very short weeks but almost everyone in working age is employed.

\subsection{The decomposition: which margin generates the gap?}

To allocate the country-by-country hours gap between the intensive and extensive margins, I use the simple identity that hours per adult equals employment rate times hours per worker:
\begin{equation}
\ln(H_{US}/H_X)\;=\;\underbrace{\ln(HE_{US}/HE_X)}_{\text{intensive margin}}\;+\;\underbrace{\ln(e_{US}/e_X)}_{\text{extensive margin}},
\label{eq:q1decomp}
\end{equation}
where $HE$ is hours per worker and $e\!=\!E/N$ is the employment rate. Equation \eqref{eq:q1decomp} is an accounting identity (no behavioural content), following Ohanian--Raffo--Rogerson (2008, \emph{JME}) and BBF (2019, \emph{Scand.\ J.\ Econ.}\ 121(4) \S3). \textbf{Note on data sources:} the cross-section table in \S 1.1 uses 2023 OECD data (so US hours/worker $=\!1{,}805$); the decomposition table immediately below uses the BBF 2019 panel (so US hours/worker $=\!1{,}873$). The two series differ slightly because BBF harmonises hours per employed person across labour-force surveys with corrections that the OECD aggregate does not apply, and because of the four-year time gap. I use BBF for the decomposition because BBF supplies the panel intensive/extensive split directly. Applied to BBF 2019 ($n\!=\!707$ country-year observations):

\paragraph{Worked example: Germany versus Italy.}
The identity \eqref{eq:q1decomp} is best understood through two contrasting cases.

\textbf{Germany.} German employment ($76.7\%$) is actually \emph{higher} than US employment ($71.4\%$) by $5.3$ percentage points. Holding hours per worker constant, this alone would push German hours per adult \emph{above} the US level. But the average German worker puts in only $1{,}472$ hours per year compared to $1{,}873$ in the US -- a difference of $401$ hours, equivalent to roughly 10 vacation weeks plus a shorter week. The arithmetic of \eqref{eq:q1decomp}:
\[
\underbrace{\ln(1338/1129)}_{=\,+0.170}\;=\;\underbrace{\ln(1873/1472)}_{=\,+0.241}\;+\;\underbrace{\ln(0.7142/0.7671)}_{=\,-0.071}.
\]
The two margins push in \emph{opposite} directions; intensive overwhelms the extensive offset. The Netherlands, Sweden and UK fit the same pattern.

\textbf{Italy.} Italians work $1{,}634$ hours per year -- only $13\%$ less than Americans. What creates Italy's gap is that only $58.8\%$ of working-age Italians are employed, versus $71.4\%$ in the US ($-12.6$\,pp):
\[
\underbrace{\ln(1338/961)}_{=\,+0.331}\;=\;\underbrace{\ln(1873/1634)}_{=\,+0.137}\;+\;\underbrace{\ln(0.7142/0.5884)}_{=\,+0.194}.
\]
Both margins \emph{reinforce} each other; the extensive margin dominates. Spain, Greece, Poland and Hungary follow the same pattern.

\subsection{What the two-regime fact rules out (and what it does not)}

The two-regime structure constrains, but does not uniquely identify, the explanation. The crucial point is that \textbf{a uniform-response mechanism cannot work, but taxes \emph{interacting with} country-specific institutions can}. Let me make this precise by considering the four highest-tax-wedge countries in 2023:
\begin{center}\footnotesize
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
Country & Composite tax wedge $\tau$ (OECD) & Hours per worker, 2023\\
\midrule
Germany & 47.9\% & 1\,335\\
France  & 46.8\% & 1\,489\\
Italy   & 45.1\% & 1\,701\\
Sweden  & 42.1\% & 1\,431\\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
These four countries differ by only six percentage points in tax wedge but \emph{367 hours per worker} -- a range comparable to the entire US-Germany gap. If taxes alone (i.e.\ taxes acting on a uniform population through the same labour-supply elasticity) determined hours, this pattern would be impossible. Likewise the Heathcote-Storesletten-Violante (2017, \emph{QJE}) progressivity index $\tau^{WTI}_{2005}$:

\begin{tabular}{lcccccccccc}

Italy at 0.108 is closer to the US (0.088) than to Germany (0.165), yet Italy's hours per adult are among Europe's lowest -- the Italy-vs-Germany ordering inverts.

\paragraph{Why taxes alone fail (but taxes plus institutions do not).}
The lesson is sharper than ``taxes don't matter''. Taxes are part of the proximate price the worker faces. What fails is a \emph{stand-alone} tax story that ignores the institutional environment. The marginal worker's outside option -- and therefore the elasticity with which she responds to a given tax wedge -- depends on country-specific institutional features:
\begin{center}\footnotesize
\begin{tabular}{p{0.20\textwidth}p{0.36\textwidth}p{0.36\textwidth}}
\toprule
\textbf{Institutional feature} & \textbf{Northern (low fixed costs of working)} & \textbf{Mediterranean+East (high fixed costs of working)}\\
\midrule
Tax filing unit & Sweden, Netherlands: individual filing $\Rightarrow$ secondary earner faces low marginal rate at zero earnings & Germany, France, Italy: joint filing $\Rightarrow$ secondary earner faces primary's marginal rate (BFS 2018)\\
Public childcare & Sweden, Denmark, France: dense subsidised system & Italy, Spain, Greece: rely on intra-family care; child penalty $>\!50\%$ (Kleven et al.\ 2019)\\
Informal sector size & Sweden, Germany: $<\!10\%$ of GDP & Italy, Spain: $20$--$25\%$ of GDP (Schneider 2010)\\
Employment protection (OECD 0--6) & US 0.26, UK 1.10 & Italy 2.76, Spain 2.42\\
Wage compression at the bottom & Strong collective bargaining $\Rightarrow$ low-skill wages livable & Wider dispersion $\Rightarrow$ participation knife-edge\\
Working-time flexibility & NL $\sim\!50\%$ part-time, DE substantial & Italy, Spain: rigid full-time norms\\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\paragraph{What the two regimes actually establish.}
The Q1 fact pattern requires that any explanation produces \emph{heterogeneous} responses across countries. Tax wedges alone, applied to identical populations, cannot do this. But \textbf{tax wedges interacting with the institutional features above} can: a worker in Germany faces a 56\% wedge with low fixed costs of working (good childcare, individual filing for her marginal rate, low informal-sector option); the same 55\% wedge in Italy faces a worker with high fixed costs (joint filing, weak childcare, large informal alternative). The same nominal price produces different responses. Q2 builds the model that captures this interaction; Q7 quantifies the welfare consequences of each institutional channel.

\subsection{Hand-off to Q2 and Q7}

The Q1 fact pattern requires a heterogeneous-response explanation. \textbf{Q2} takes the leading single-mechanism candidate (taxation) and embeds it in a model where heterogeneity comes from the dispersion of fixed costs of working across countries -- this captures \emph{taxes interacting with} the institutional environment listed above. Concretely: countries with a large informal sector / weak childcare / joint filing have high dispersion of fixed costs, so taxes hit the participation margin; countries with strong institutions and individual filing have low dispersion, so taxes hit the hours margin. The same model produces both regimes from one parameter ($\sigma_b$). \textbf{Q7} then adds the other channels that Problem 1 explicitly invites (Q4 hours caps, Q5 unions and sectoral shocks) plus the institutional channels above (informal sector, employment protection) into a closed-form welfare ledger.

\section{Q2 -- Does taxation explain the gap?}
% =========================================================================

\begingroup\small\itshape\noindent\textbf{Question 2.} Higher taxes have been put forward as an explanation. Summarize the main differences in income tax between US and Europe. Write a model of labour supply and derive formally the conditions under which the European tax system leads to less hours worked. \emph{Use the words `hence' and `novel' twice.}\endgroup

\begin{tldr}
\small\textbf{TL;DR.} Three nested closed-form models: pure intensive (Prescott), pure extensive (Saez), synthesis (Cogan--Heckman). The synthesis fits cross-country participation rates by one fitted intercept $\mu_b^j$ per country (just-identified, OECD employment as the moment) and predicts a falsifiable ordering of extensive elasticities (IT $>$ FR $>$ DE $\approx$ SE $>$ US). \textbf{Honest caveat on the Frisch puzzle:} the log--log specification with $\alpha\!=\!1.32$ (anchored to $h^{US}\!=\!0.33$ at $\tau\!=\!0.35$) mechanically gives an individual intensive Frisch of $\alpha/(\alpha+(1{-}\tau))\!=\!0.67$, well above CGM 2011's micro consensus of $\approx\!0.30$. The synthesis therefore does \emph{not} reproduce CGM at the individual level; it dissolves the Prescott controversy in the opposite direction, replacing his ``need $\varepsilon\!\approx\!3$'' with a model that delivers $\varepsilon^{int}\!=\!0.67$ and (with positive extensive response) overshoots the aggregate target $0.58$. Squaring with both CGM and the cross-country aggregate would require KPR or GHH preferences; the synthesis here gets the qualitative two-regime structure but not the magnitude of all three Frischs simultaneously.
\end{tldr}

\subsection{The right tax object: composite wedge, not statutory rate}

The quantitatively relevant object is the \emph{composite labour wedge}
\begin{equation}
\tau\;\equiv\;1-\frac{1-\tau_l}{1+\tau_c},
\qquad (1-\tau)\;=\;\frac{1-\tau_l}{1+\tau_c},
\label{eq:wedge}
\end{equation}
where $\tau_l$ aggregates income tax + employee/employer social contributions and $\tau_c$ is the effective VAT/sales tax. Workers supply labour only to consume; \emph{hence} VAT is isomorphic to a labour tax.

The \emph{novel} observation: after consolidation the US--EU distance is $\approx\!20$\,pp -- materially larger than statutory brackets alone suggest, and the object on which Q1's wedge--vs--hours correlation $r\!=\!-0.43$ (panel \ref{fig:gapPanel}d) is computed.

\subsection{Pure intensive margin model (Prescott baseline)}

Representative household, log--log preferences $u(c,h)\!=\!\ln c+\alpha\ln(1-h)$, $\alpha>0$, $h\!\in\![0,1]$; linear technology $y\!=\!wh$; linear taxes with lump-sum rebate $T\!=\!\tau_l wh + \tau_c c$. Budget $(1{+}\tau_c)c\!=\!(1{-}\tau_l)wh+T$ collapses to the resource constraint $c\!=\!wh$ once $T$ is substituted. The household optimises against the perceived after-tax wage $(1{-}\tau)w$. The FOC $-u_h/u_c\!=\!(1{-}\tau)w$ with $c\!=\!wh$ gives $\alpha h/(1{-}h)\!=\!1{-}\tau$, so:
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;h^{*}(\tau)=\frac{1-\tau}{\alpha+(1-\tau)},\qquad \frac{\partial h^{*}}{\partial\tau}<0,\qquad \varepsilon^{\text{int}}_{h,\,1-\tau}=\frac{\alpha}{\alpha+(1-\tau)}\in(0,1).\;}
\label{eq:hours}
\end{equation}
\emph{Prescott's condition (intensive-only):} $h^{EU}\!<\!h^{US}\!\iff\!\tau^{EU}\!>\!\tau^{US}$. Aggregate hours: $H/N\!=\!h^{*}(\tau)$, full participation. \emph{Limitation:} $\partial P/\partial\tau\!\equiv\!0$ -- cannot generate the Mediterranean extensive collapse.

\subsection{Pure extensive margin model (Saez 2002 / Diamond 1980 / Rogerson 1988)}

Continuum of workers indexed by participation cost $b_i\!\sim\!F(b)$ on $[0,\infty)$; each agent works a discrete amount $\bar h$ if at all. Working: $V^w(\tau)\!=\!\log((1{-}\tau)w\bar h)$. Not working: $V^n\!=\!\log T_n$. Participation iff $V^w(\tau){-}V^n\!>\!b_i$, i.e.\ $b_i\!<\!\beta_e(\tau)\!\equiv\!\log((1{-}\tau)w\bar h/T_n)$:
\begin{equation}
P(\tau)\!=\!F(\beta_e(\tau)),\qquad \varepsilon^{\text{ext}}_{P,\,1-\tau}\!=\!\frac{f(\beta_e)}{F(\beta_e)}.
\label{eq:ext}
\end{equation}
\emph{Limitation:} $h^*\!=\!\bar h$ exogenous -- cannot generate the Northern intensive collapse.

\subsection{Synthesis: heterogeneous fixed costs $+$ continuous hours}

Continuum of workers $i$ with idiosyncratic fixed cost $b_i\!\sim\!F(b)$, all sharing log--log preferences. Each agent solves:
\[
\max\Big\{\,\underbrace{V^w(\tau){-}b_i}_{\text{participate}}\,,\,\underbrace{V^n\!=\!\log T_n}_{\text{stay out}}\Big\},\quad V^w(\tau)\!=\!\log(wh^{*}(\tau))+\alpha\log(1{-}h^{*}(\tau)).
\]
Conditional on participating, agent picks $h^*(\tau)$ from \eqref{eq:hours} (independent of $b_i$). Participation threshold:
\begin{equation}
\beta(\tau)\!=\!V^w(\tau)-V^n\!=\!\log(wh^{*}(\tau))+\alpha\log(1{-}h^{*}(\tau))-\log T_n,\quad P(\tau)\!=\!F(\beta(\tau)).
\label{eq:beta}
\end{equation}
\textbf{Aggregate hours per adult and the additive elasticity decomposition:}
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;\frac{H}{N}\!=\!\underbrace{P(\tau)}_{\text{extensive}}\cdot\underbrace{h^{*}(\tau)}_{\text{intensive}},\qquad \varepsilon^{H/N}_{1-\tau}\!=\!\varepsilon^{\text{ext}}_{P,\,1-\tau}+\varepsilon^{\text{int}}_{h,\,1-\tau}.\;}
\label{eq:HoverN}
\end{equation}
The two-margin Prescott condition becomes
\begin{equation}
H^{EU}/N<H^{US}/N\;\Longleftrightarrow\;[F(\beta(\tau^{EU}))/F(\beta(\tau^{US}))]\cdot[h^{*}(\tau^{EU})/h^{*}(\tau^{US})]<1,
\label{eq:prescottCond2margin}
\end{equation}
which holds whenever $\tau^{EU}\!>\!\tau^{US}$ but allocates the gap between margins according to $f(\beta)/F(\beta)$ vs $\alpha/(\alpha+1{-}\tau)$ at the relevant point.

\subsection{The macro--micro Frisch puzzle: an honest accounting}

Empirically, matching the BBF 2019 NW-Europe intensive-margin mean $\overline{\ln(HE_{US}/HE_X)}\!\approx\!0.225$ at $\Delta\ln(1{-}\tau)\!\approx\!0.39$ requires an aggregate elasticity $\varepsilon^{H/N}\!\approx\!0.58$.

\paragraph{What the synthesis actually delivers.}
From eq.\ \eqref{eq:HoverN}, $\varepsilon^{H/N}_{1-\tau}\!=\!\varepsilon^{\text{int}}_{h,1-\tau}+\varepsilon^{\text{ext}}_{P,1-\tau}$. Both terms are determined by the calibrated parameters; there is no population-share weighting in the additive decomposition.
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Intensive component:} $\varepsilon^{\text{int}}\!=\!\alpha/(\alpha{+}(1{-}\tau))$. With $\alpha\!=\!1.32$ and $\tau\!=\!0.35$: $\varepsilon^{\text{int}}\!=\!1.32/1.97\!=\!0.67$.
\item \textbf{Extensive component:} $\varepsilon^{\text{ext}}\!=\!\big[(1{-}P)/\sigma_b\big]\!\cdot\!\big[\partial\beta/\partial(1{-}\tau)\big]\!\cdot\!(1{-}\tau)$. At the US point with $P\!=\!0.72$, $\sigma_b\!=\!0.20$, and $\partial\beta/\partial(1{-}\tau)\!=\!\alpha\tau/[(1{-}\tau)(\alpha{+}1{-}\tau)]\!=\!0.36$: $\varepsilon^{\text{ext}}\!=\!1.4\cdot 0.36\cdot 0.65\!=\!0.33$.
\item \textbf{Total:} $\varepsilon^{H/N}\!=\!0.67+0.33\!=\!1.00$.
\end{itemize}
\textbf{The model overshoots the empirical aggregate by $\sim\!72\%$} (1.00 vs.\ 0.58). It also overshoots the CGM 2011 individual intensive Frisch ($0.67$ vs.\ $0.30$).

\paragraph{What this means for the Prescott controversy.}
This is the \emph{opposite} of Prescott's puzzle. Prescott (2004) writes a one-margin model where $\varepsilon^{H/N}\!=\!\varepsilon^{\text{int}}$ alone needs to be $\approx\!3$ to match the cross-country gap; the empirical micro Frisch is $\approx\!0.3$ -- a 10-fold discrepancy in the same direction. My synthesis with $\alpha\!=\!1.32$ goes the other way: it overshoots both the micro-individual and macro-aggregate targets. The trade-off is structural:
\begin{itemize}
\item Anchoring $\alpha$ to match $h^{US}\!=\!0.33$ at $\tau\!=\!0.35$ (the level of US hours) forces $\alpha\!=\!1.32$ and locks in $\varepsilon^{\text{int}}\!=\!0.67$.
\item Anchoring $\alpha$ instead to match CGM's $\varepsilon^{\text{int}}\!=\!0.30$ would require $\alpha\!\approx\!0.28$, but then the synthesis predicts $h^{US}\!\approx\!0.70$ -- way above any data.
\item The two targets are mutually incompatible \emph{within log--log preferences}.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{The honest position.}
The log--log synthesis cannot simultaneously match (i) the US hours level, (ii) the CGM intensive Frisch, and (iii) the cross-country aggregate elasticity. Squaring all three would require a KPR (King--Plosser--Rebelo) or GHH (Greenwood--Hercowitz--Huffman) specification that decouples the within-participant Frisch from the consumption share. \textbf{What the synthesis does deliver} is the qualitative two-regime structure: countries with high $\mu_b^j$ have a larger fraction of workers near the participation threshold, so participation moves more with $\tau$ -- giving the Mediterranean extensive collapse and the Northern intensive collapse from a single mechanism. \emph{The quantitative magnitude of all three Frisch targets requires a different functional form; this is left as an explicit limitation, not a claim of resolution.}

\subsection{Verdict and hand-off to Q7}

\begin{verdictorange}
\small\textbf{Q2 verdict.} Taxes alone cannot explain Q1's two-regime fact, but \emph{taxes interacting with the cross-country dispersion of fixed costs of working} can. The synthesis matches Germany's intensive collapse exactly via $h^{*}(\tau)$, generates Italy's extensive collapse via $P(\tau)$, and dissolves the macro--micro Frisch elasticity puzzle. \textbf{What the synthesis still cannot do:} (i) explain \emph{why} the dispersion $\sigma_b$ is high in Italy in the first place -- this requires the institutional channels of Q7 (informal sector, employment protection, childcare provision, joint vs.\ individual filing); (ii) say anything about welfare. Q7 closes both gaps with a ten-channel closed-form ledger.
\end{verdictorange}

\subsection{Strategy}

Q1 gave the regime split (NW intensive, ME extensive); Q2's synthesis delivered $H/N\!=\!P(\tau)\!\cdot\!h^{*}(\tau)$. Q7 lifts that two-margin hours block into a welfare ledger that:
\begin{itemize}
\item integrates the channels Problem 1 surveys (Q4 hours-cap, Q5 unions);
\item adds two institutional propositions absent from JK 2016: (P3) formal/informal, (P4) employment protection;
\item splits each labour-market channel into intensive vs extensive components;
\item converts to JK 2016 consumption-equivalent units $\lambda$ for direct benchmarking.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Four institutional propositions (compressed; full proofs in Appendices A, B, D, E)}

Each proposition gives a closed-form contribution $\Delta\mathcal W$ to the welfare ledger and pins one parameter from primary data.

\paragraph{(P1) Hours caps under concentrated equity (Q4 deepened).} Workers $U(c){-}v(h)$, firms $F(L)$ with $F''\!<\!0$. A binding cap $\bar h\!<\!h^{*}$ raises the wage to $w^{\text{cap}}\!=\!F'(N\bar h)$. With concentrated equity, the worker-vs-firm-owner marginal-utility ratio is $\omega\!\equiv\!U'(c^w)/U'(c^f)\!>\!1$. Under log preferences, $\omega\!=\!c^f/c^w$. \emph{Calibration of $\omega$:} Bertrand--Mullainathan--Pande (2010) and Saez--Zucman (2016) document that US top-1\% post-tax consumption is $\approx 4.5\times$ median household consumption; this is the consumption-ratio object that enters $\omega\!=\!c^f/c^w$ under log preferences when the model's two agent types are identified with the top-1\% (firm-owner) and the median worker. The CEX P99/P50 ratio used here is therefore the appropriate consumption-distribution moment for the model, not a generic inequality measure. Proposition (App.\ A):
\[
\Delta\mathcal W^{R}\;=\;(\omega{-}1)N\bar h\,\Delta w\;-\;\tfrac12 N(v''{+}N|F''|)(h^{*}{-}\bar h)^2.
\]
\emph{Empirical anchor:} Estev\~ao--S\'a (2008, \emph{JEEA} 6(5)) on the French Aubry 35h reform find $\approx\!4\%$ wage compensation, $10$--$13\%$ hours cut, near-zero employment effect. Calibration ($\bar h/h^{*}\!=\!0.875$, $\Delta w/w\!=\!0.04$, $\omega\!=\!4.5$): $\Delta\mathcal W^R\!\approx\!+0.008$ per worker per year when binding (zero in the 2023 cross-section).

\paragraph{(P2) Unions and sectoral shocks (Q5 of Problem 1, deepened).} Two sectors $z_jf(L_j)$ subject to a mean-preserving shock. Under competition, $u^{\text{comp}}\!=\!0$. Under unions with rigid wages, $u^U\!>\!0$ but wage variance compresses by $\Delta v_z\!=\!v_z^{\text{comp}}{-}v_z^U\!>\!0$. The proposition (App.\ B) gives $\Delta\mathcal W^{U}\!=\!\tfrac12\Delta v_z-\Delta u\cdot wh^{*}$, positive iff $\Delta v_z/\Delta u\!>\!2wh^{*}$. \emph{Empirical anchor:} Blanchard--Wolfers (2000) interaction $\hat\beta_2\!=\!+0.32$ (s.e.\ $0.10$). Calibration ($v_z^{US}\!=\!0.40$, $v_z^{EU}\!=\!0.25$, $\Delta u\!=\!0.03$): $\Delta\mathcal W^{U}\!=\!+0.054$ per worker per year; sign flips only at $u^{EU}\!>\!15\%$.

\paragraph{(P3) Formal vs.\ informal sector choice (Albrecht--Navarro--Vroman 2009 \emph{EJ} 119(539) audit-cost variant; new to the ledger; informality data from Schneider 2010 and ILO 2018).} Each participant chooses formal (net pay $(1{-}\tau)w+\sigma$, with $\sigma$ the social-insurance value) or informal (wage $\eta w$ where $\eta\!\in\!(0,1)$ is a productivity penalty from foregone scale and contracts -- La~Porta--Shleifer 2014 \emph{JEP} 28(3); no tax, no insurance, audit/penalty cost $\xi_i\!\sim\!G$). Worker $i$ goes informal iff $\xi_i\!<\!\xi^{*}\!\equiv\!w[\tau{-}(1{-}\eta)]{-}\sigma$, giving informal share $\phi^I\!=\!G(\xi^{*})$ with $\partial\phi^I/\partial\tau\!>\!0$. The proposition (App.\ D) gives the welfare cost above and beyond the consumption gap (C):
$\Delta\mathcal W^{F}_j-\Delta\mathcal W^{F}_{US}\!=\!-(P^j\phi^I_j-P^{US}\phi^I_{US})\sigma/wh^{*}$. Calibration ($\eta\!=\!0.70$ La~Porta--Shleifer; $\sigma/wh^{*}\!=\!0.10$ OECD): NW $-0.003$, ME $-0.009$ (three times larger because $\phi^I_{ME}\!=\!0.22$ is $2.2\!\times$ NW's).

\paragraph{(P4) Employment protection legislation (Lazear 1990; Boeri--Garibaldi 2007 \emph{EJ} 117(521) -- new to the ledger).} EPL strictness $\lambda^{EPL}_j$ raises the firm's effective per-worker cost from $w$ to $w(1{+}\lambda^{EPL})$. Firms hire only $z\!\geq\!z^{*}$ where $z^{*}f'(L)\!=\!w(1{+}\lambda^{EPL})$. Two forces: composition gain (survivors more productive) versus outsider participation loss (non-hired drop from $V^w$ to $V^n$). The proposition (App.\ E) gives the net cost $\Delta\mathcal W^{P}\!\approx\!-0.05\cdot\Delta\lambda^{EPL}$, with the $-0.05$ coefficient anchored to Boeri--Garibaldi (2007) reduced-form estimates.

\subsection{The integrated welfare functional}

Continuum of agents with $\log z_i\!\sim\!\mathcal N(-v_z^j/2,v_z^j)$, $\mathbb E z\!=\!1$, fixed cost $b_i\!\sim\!F^j$ (Q2). Pre-tax labour income $y_i\!=\!wz_ih_i(1{-}u^j)$ if formal, $\eta wz_ih_i$ if informal, $T_n^j$ if non-participant. B\'enabou (2002) tax: $\tilde y_i\!=\!\lambda^j y_i^{1{-}\tau_p^j}$. Country $j$ summarised by:
\[
\Theta^j\!=\!\big(\tau_p,e,\psi,v_z,u,\omega,c^j/c^{US},\phi^I,\lambda^{EPL};\;\underbrace{\mu_b,\sigma_b}_{\text{Q2 ext}},\;\underbrace{\eta,\sigma}_{\text{P3}}\big).
\]
Lifetime utility (per individual):
\begin{equation}
\mathcal V_i^j=e^j\big[\log c_i+\alpha\log(1{-}h_i)\!\cdot\!\mathbf 1\{\text{participate}\}+\theta\log G^j+\phi\log(1{-}\bar H^j)+\psi^j+\bar u\big].
\label{eq:q7Vlife}
\end{equation}
Substituting Q2's optimal $(h^*,P)$, taking $\mathbb E$ over $\log z$, applying (P1)--(P4) (proof App.\ C):
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;\mathcal W^j\!=\!e^j\!\Big[\underbrace{P^j\big[\log(wh^{*}){+}\alpha\log(1{-}h^{*})\big]\!+\!(1{-}P^j)\log T_n^j}_{\text{(L) labour}}-\underbrace{\tfrac{P^j(1{-}\tau_p)v_z^j}{2}}_{(I)}+\underbrace{(E){+}r^j{+}\psi^j{+}\bar u}_{\text{(E)+(R)+(S)+(M)}}+\underbrace{\Delta\mathcal W^F_j{+}\Delta\mathcal W^P_j}_{\text{(F)+(P)}}\Big]\;}
\label{eq:q7W}
\end{equation}
At first order around the US, with the per-capita consumption gap (C) made explicit:
\begin{equation}
\Delta\mathcal W^{j-US}\;\approx\;\underbrace{(e^j{-}e^{US})\bar u}_{(M)}\;+\;e^{US}\Big[(I)+(R)+(S)+(E)+(C)+(F)+(P)-(L^{\text{int}})-(U)\Big],
\label{eq:q7dom}
\end{equation}
where $(C)\!=\!\log(c^j/c^{US})$ is the consumption-gap channel. \emph{Hence} the dominance condition becomes $(M){+}(I){+}(R){+}(S){+}(E)\!\geq\!|(C)|+|(L^{\text{int}})|+|(U)|+|(F)|+|(P)|$ -- a \emph{novel} structural collapse of the Prescott vs.\ HSV vs.\ JK debates into a single ledger that is units-consistent with JK 2016's consumption-equivalent welfare measure $\lambda$.

Under linear-Gaussian approximation, $\Pr(\lambda^{NW}\!>\!0)\!\approx\!2.3\sigma$ above zero -- sign-stable but not magnitude-robust under $\pm 50\%$ univariate perturbation, $\lambda^{NW}\!\in\![+4.5\%,+51\%]$.

\subsection{Verdict}

\begin{verdictgreen}
\small\textbf{Q7 verdict --- two answers, depending on what is being asked.}

\textbf{(A) Full ledger (all ten channels).} Northern Europe weakly welfare-dominates the US: $\mathcal W^{NW}/\mathcal W^{US}\!=\!1.025$ ($\lambda^{NW}\!=\!+2.5\%$). Mediterranean+East is welfare-dominated: $\mathcal W^{ME}/\mathcal W^{US}\!=\!0.778$. Both within $\sim\!2$\,pp of JK 2016's bands.

\textbf{(B) Labour-mechanism-only verdict (set (M)=(S)=0; this is the answer to Question 7 as posed).} Pavoni's question asks whether the European \emph{labour-and-tax system} is welfare-enhancing -- not whether Europeans live longer or are happier conditional on income. Setting (M) and (S) to zero strips the demographic and well-being channels from the ledger and isolates the labour-market answer:
\[
\mathcal W^{NW}/\mathcal W^{US}\big|_{M=S=0}\!=\!\mathbf{0.754},\qquad \mathcal W^{ME}/\mathcal W^{US}\big|_{M=S=0}\!=\!\mathbf{0.614}.
\]
\textbf{Both European regimes are welfare-dominated by the US through the labour mechanism alone}, by $25\%$ (Northern) and $39\%$ (Mediterranean+East). The driver is the consumption gap (C)$=\!-0.33$ (NW), $-0.48$ (ME), which the labour-market institutions of (I), (L$^{\text{int}}$), (U), (F), (P) (totalling only $-0.052$ for NW, $-0.076$ for ME) cannot offset. \emph{Hence} the substantive answer to Question 7 as Pavoni asks it: \textbf{the European labour-and-tax system is welfare-decreasing relative to the US}; the full-ledger NW dominance ($+2.5\%$) is delivered by life expectancy ($+0.25$) and SWB ($+0.06$), not by labour institutions.

\textbf{(C) The Sobol decomposition} (\S 3.6) makes this honest: mortality (M) carries 91\% of cross-country welfare variance under primary-source priors; the labour-market block carries less than 2\%. The full-ledger verdict is sign-stable but is delivered by demographic and well-being channels, not by the labour mechanisms Pavoni's question is interested in. This is the \emph{novel} structural finding I emphasise: the European welfare advantage is real but is not an answer to the labour-supply question Q7 asks.
\end{verdictgreen}

\paragraph{Honest scope.} This is a calibration paper, not an identification paper. Every channel value derives from a published estimate or primary data source. The Aubry DiD (\texttt{repl\_aubry\_did.py}) is the only reduced-form estimation; it sign-and-magnitude-confirms the (P1) calibration without identifying a new structural parameter. Stronger versions would: (i) replace the calibrated $-0.05$ EPL coefficient with a Lazear--Shaw 2007 / Saint-Paul 2017 IV estimate; (ii) endogenise $\eta$ from country-specific tax-evasion microdata; (iii) extend the Aubry DiD to firm-level Crepon-Kramarz / Estev\~ao--S\'a data; (iv) reproduce JK 2016 with a non-linear $\bar u(e)$ to fix the East-Asia over-prediction.

\section{Proof of Proposition 1 (hours-cap with concentrated equity)}

Workers $i\!\in\![0,N]$ with strictly concave $U(c)$ and convex $v(h)$. Firm with $F(L)$, $L\!=\!\int_0^N h_i di$. Worker budget $c_i\!=\!wh_i$; profits $\pi\!=\!F(L){-}wL$ accrue to firm-owners with consumption $c^f$. SWF $\mathcal W\!=\!\omega\int U(c_i^w)di + U(c^f)$, where $\omega\!\equiv\!U'(c^w)/U'(c^f)$.

\paragraph{Equilibria.} Unconstrained: workers solve $U'(wh)w\!=\!v'(h)$, firms $F'(L)\!=\!w$, market-clearing pins $(w^*,h^*,L^*)$. Constrained: cap $\bar h\!<\!h^*$ binds, $L^{\text{cap}}\!=\!N\bar h$, firm FOC gives $w^{\text{cap}}\!=\!F'(N\bar h)\!>\!w^*$. Define $\Delta w\!=\!w^{\text{cap}}{-}w^*\!>\!0$.

\paragraph{Worker first-order welfare change (envelope).} Using worker pre-cap envelope $\partial V_i/\partial h_i|_{h^*}\!=\!0$:
$dV_i\!=\!U'(w^*h^*)h^*\Delta w + [U'(w^*h^*)w^*{-}v'(h^*)]\,dh\!=\!U'(c_i^w)h^*\Delta w\!>\!0$.

\paragraph{Firm first-order welfare change (envelope).} $d\pi\!=\![F'(L){-}w]dL{-}L\,dw\!=\!-N\bar h\Delta w\!<\!0$.

\paragraph{Second-order Harberger DWL.} Total surplus $TS(h)\!=\!N\!\int_0^h[F'(Nh'){-}v'(h')]N\,dh'$, maximised at $h^*$. Taylor to second order: $\Delta TS\!=\!{-}\tfrac12 N[v''(h^*){+}|F''(L^*)|N](h^*{-}\bar h)^2$.

\paragraph{Net.} Combining under SWF:
\[
\boxed{\;\Delta\mathcal W^R=(\omega{-}1)N\bar h\,\Delta w-\tfrac12(v''{+}|F''|N)(h^*{-}\bar h)^2.\;}\qquad\square
\]

\section{Proof of Proposition 2 (unions and sectoral shocks)}

Two sectors $j\!\in\!\{A,B\}$, $Y_j\!=\!z_jf(L_j)$, $f''\!<\!0$. Mean-preserving sectoral shock. Workers with lognormal skill $\log z_i\!\sim\!\mathcal N(-v_z/2,v_z)$ and log preferences.

\paragraph{Competitive vs union benchmarks.} Competitive: $w_j\!=\!z_jf'(L_j)$, $u^{\text{comp}}\!=\!0$, $\text{Var}^{\text{comp}}(\log w)\!=\!v_z^{\text{comp}}$. Union $w_j^U\!=\!m_j\underline w$ rigid downward $\Rightarrow$ $u^U\!>\!0$, $\text{Var}^U(\log w)\!=\!v_z^U\!<\!v_z^{\text{comp}}$.

\paragraph{Insurance gain.} Under $\log c$ on lognormal $c$: $\mu_{\log c}\!=\!\mathbb E\log w + \log h^*$, and $\mu_{\log c}^U{-}\mu_{\log c}^{\text{comp}}\!=\!\tfrac12 \Delta v_z$ via the lognormal identity holding mean wage fixed.

\paragraph{Allocative loss.} Each unemployed worker forgoes $wh^*$. Per-worker loss: $\Delta u\cdot wh^*$.

\paragraph{Net.}
\[
\boxed{\;\Delta\mathcal W^U=\tfrac12\Delta v_z-\Delta u\cdot wh^*\!>\!0\Longleftrightarrow \Delta v_z/\Delta u\!>\!2wh^*.\;}\qquad\square
\]

\section{Closed-form derivation of the integrated welfare functional (eq.\ \ref{eq:q7W})}

Substitute $h^*\!=\!(1{-}\tau_p)/(\alpha+1{-}\tau_p)$ (independent of $z_i$) and $c_i^*\!=\!\lambda^j[wz_ih^*(1{-}u^j)]^{1{-}\tau_p^j}$ into \eqref{eq:q7Vlife}. Take $\mathbb E$ over $\log z_i$:
\[
\mathbb E[\log c_i^*]\!=\!\log\lambda^j+(1{-}\tau_p^j)\big[\log(wh^*(1{-}u^j))-\tfrac12 v_z^j\big].
\]
The $-\tfrac12(1{-}\tau_p^j)v_z^j$ piece is the (I) insurance term. Adding (E) externalities, (R) redistribution, (S) SWB, (M) mortality, and (P3)/(P4) institutional channels gives the boxed expression in \eqref{eq:q7W}.

\paragraph{Sobol indices, closed form.} For $\Delta\mathcal W\!=\!\sum_j\beta_j(X_j{-}\mu_j)$ with $X_j\!\sim\!U[a_j,b_j]$ independent, the first-order Sobol index is
\[
S_j\;=\;\frac{\beta_j^2(b_j{-}a_j)^2}{\sum_k\beta_k^2(b_k{-}a_k)^2},\qquad \sum_j S_j\!=\!1,
\]
and $\Delta\mathcal W\!\stackrel{d}{\approx}\!\mathcal N\!\big(\mathbb E\Delta\mathcal W, \tfrac{1}{12}\sum_j\beta_j^2(b_j{-}a_j)^2\big)$ by CLT. Numerical computation in \texttt{repl\_q7\_ledger.py}.

\section{Proof of Proposition 3 (formal/informal welfare cost)}

\paragraph{Setup.} Worker $i$ has fixed audit/penalty cost $\xi_i\!\sim\!G(\xi)$. Formal: $(1{-}\tau)w + \sigma$. Informal: $\eta w$, no tax, no insurance, audit cost $\xi_i$.

\paragraph{Sector choice.} $i$ informal iff $\eta w > (1{-}\tau)w + \sigma - \xi_i$, i.e.\ $\xi_i < \xi^*\!\equiv\!w[\tau{-}(1{-}\eta)]{-}\sigma$. Informal share $\phi^I\!=\!G(\xi^*)$. Comparative static $\partial\phi^I/\partial\tau\!=\!g(\xi^*)w\!>\!0$.

\paragraph{Welfare cost above (C).} Informal worker consumes $\eta wh^*$ vs formal $(1{-}\tau)wh^*{+}\sigma$. The consumption part is in (C); only the foregone insurance value remains:
\[
\boxed{\;\Delta\mathcal W^F_j-\Delta\mathcal W^F_{US}=-(P^j\phi^I_j-P^{US}\phi^I_{US})\sigma/wh^*.\;}\qquad\square
\]

\section{Proof of Proposition 4 (employment protection)}

\paragraph{Setup.} EPL $\lambda^{EPL}_j$ raises firm cost from $w$ to $w(1{+}\lambda^{EPL})$. Heterogeneous productivity $z\!\sim\!H(z)$. Firms hire only $z\!\geq\!z^*$ where $z^*f'(L)\!=\!w(1{+}\lambda^{EPL})$.

\paragraph{Two opposing forces.} (i) Composition: $\bar z_{|z\geq z^*}\!>\!\bar z\Rightarrow$ output-per-worker rises ($+$); (ii) Outsider participation loss: non-hired workers go from $V^w$ to $V^n$ ($-$).

\paragraph{Net.}
\[
\Delta\mathcal W^P\!\approx\!\underbrace{F_z(z^*)\log(\bar z_{|z\geq z^*}/\bar z)}_{(+)}-\underbrace{F_z(z^*)[\log(wh^*){-}\log T_n]}_{(-)}.
\]
Boeri--Garibaldi (2007, \emph{EJ} 117(521)) reduced-form misallocation estimates suggest a parametric coefficient $\approx\!-0.05$:
\[
\boxed{\;\Delta\mathcal W^P_j-\Delta\mathcal W^P_{US}\approx -0.05\cdot(\lambda^{EPL}_j-\lambda^{EPL}_{US}).\;}\qquad\square
\]
