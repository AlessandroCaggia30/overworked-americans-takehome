\section*{Q1 -- The US--Europe hours gap: primary-source evidence}
%====================================================================

\begingroup\small\itshape\noindent\textbf{Question.} Document the fact described above about the difference between hours worked in the US vs Europe in recent years, if needed even across different European countries.\endgroup

\paragraph{Approach.}
Treating every narrative as a hypothesis, I work from primary data: OECD annual hrs/worker (\texttt{DSD\_HW}, 1950--2024); OECD employment rate 15--64 (\texttt{DSD\_LFS\_EMP\_WAP\_Q}, quarterly); OECD tax wedge (\texttt{DSD\_TAX\_WAGES\_COMP}); and the Bick--Br\"uggemann--Fuchs-Sch\"undeln (2019, \emph{Scandinavian J.\ Econ.}\ 121(4); hereafter \textbf{BBF}) Data Release 3 replication archive (707 obs, 1983--2019). I verified Kleven--Landais--Posch--Steinhauer--Zweim\"uller (2019, \emph{AEA P\&P}; NBER WP 25524) child-penalty numbers from the WP PDF. Every numerical claim carries a [SOURCE:\ ...] tag; computations are reproducible from \texttt{research/}.

\paragraph{Headline fact.}
Hours per working-age adult is \emph{lower} in NW European countries than the US; the gap is recent (post-1970), largely concentrated on women, and \emph{not} a uniform ``Europe vs US'' phenomenon. Mediterranean and Eastern European countries out-work the US on hrs/worker.

\paragraph{Accounting identity.}
$H/N \;=\; (E/N)\cdot HE \;=\; (E/N)\cdot (D\cdot h)$, where $E/N$ is the employment rate 15--64, $HE$ is annual hours per employed, $D$ is weeks worked, $h$ is weekly hours per employed (Ohanian--Raffo--Rogerson 2008, \emph{JME}; BBF notation).

\paragraph{Disconfirmation of monocausal tax story (panel f).}
The four highest-$\tau$ OECD countries in the 2023 panel --- DEU 47.9\%, FRA 46.8\%, ITA 45.1\%, SWE 42.1\% [SOURCE: OECD \texttt{DSD\_TAX\_WAGES\_COMP}] --- post hrs/worker of 1\,335 / 1\,489 / 1\,701 / 1\,431. \textbf{A 367-hour spread within a 6-pp $\tau$ band} falsifies a Prescott-style monocausal tax explanation.

\paragraph{Solid vs.\ unresolved.}
\textbf{Solid (multi-source, replicated):} (i) lower hrs/adult in NW Europe vs US; (ii) gap is post-1970, Continental decline-driven; (iii) gap is concentrated on women, not on prime-age men; (iv) child-penalty cross-country range 21--61\% (Kleven et al.\ 2019); (v) total work (market $+$ home) approximately equalised across rich countries (Burda--Hamermesh--Weil 2013).\\
\textbf{Unresolved:} (i) macro Frisch needed for tax-only story ($\approx 3$) is 5--10$\times$ micro consensus (Chetty et al.\ 2011); aggregation closes part but not all; (ii) child-penalty cross-country variation: norms (Kleven et al.\ 2019, ESS-based), joint taxation (Bick--Fuchs-Sch\"undeln 2018), or childcare (Olivetti--Petrongolo 2017) --- no joint horse-race; (iii) why Italy/Greece pattern with US on hrs/worker but with NW Europe on hrs/adult lacks a unified model. \textbf{Open question worth a follow-up:} joint estimation of (norms / joint $\tau$ / childcare / parental leave) contributions to long-run child penalties using Kleven Atlas (2024 \emph{RES}) micro data $+$ BBF tax-code archive, with quasi-experimental variation from staggered childcare reforms.

\section*{Q2 -- Labour supply, the composite wedge, and Prescott's condition}
%====================================================================

\begingroup\small\itshape\noindent\textbf{Question.} One explanation that has been put forward --- for example, by Ed Prescott in 2004 (`Why do Americans work so much more than Europeans?') --- is higher taxes. Summarize the main differences in the income tax between US and Europe (or a few European countries). \emph{Use the words `hence' and `novel' twice in your answer.} Write down a model of labor supply and derive formally the conditions under which the European tax system will lead to less hours worked than in the US. [Hint: start with the simplest case of differences only in the average tax rates.]\endgroup

\paragraph{Modelling choice, trade-offs, limitations.}
I adopt a one-period representative-agent model with balanced-growth preferences (log in consumption, log in leisure) and a linear labour tax with lump-sum revenue rebate. This captures exactly the trade-off the question is about: the substitution between market work and leisure under a wedge, with income effects neutralised by the rebate so that cross-country variation isolates the pure price response. I ignore --- for transparency --- dynamics, heterogeneity, home production, and GE wage responses; Q3 relaxes the first three and Q7 the last.

\paragraph{US vs.\ Europe income tax in one object.}
The quantitatively relevant object is not the statutory marginal income tax but the \emph{composite labour wedge}
\begin{equation}
\tau\;\equiv\;1-\frac{1-\tau_{l}}{1+\tau_{c}},
\qquad
(1-\tau)\;=\;\frac{1-\tau_{l}}{1+\tau_{c}},
\label{eq:wedge}
\end{equation}
where $\tau_{l}$ aggregates income tax + employee/employer social contributions and $\tau_{c}$ is the effective consumption tax (VAT/sales). A worker supplies labour only to consume: VAT is isomorphic to a labour tax. Hence the correct cross-country comparison aggregates both instruments.

\begin{center}
\footnotesize
\begin{tabular}{lcccc}
\toprule
Country & $\tau_{l}$ (OECD wedge) & Top stat.\ rate & Std.\ VAT & Composite $\tau$\\
\midrule
United States  & 0.30 & 0.37 (fed) & 0.07 (sales, avg.) & $\approx 0.35$\\
Germany        & 0.48 & 0.45 & 0.19 & $\approx 0.56$\\
France         & 0.47 & 0.45 & 0.20 & $\approx 0.56$\\
Italy          & 0.45 & 0.43 & 0.22 & $\approx 0.55$\\
United Kingdom & 0.31 & 0.45 & 0.20 & $\approx 0.42$\\
Sweden         & 0.42 & 0.52 & 0.25 & $\approx 0.54$\\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\quad\emph{Source:} OECD \emph{Taxing Wages} (single, avg.-wage worker); national VAT.
\end{center}
The \emph{novel} observation relative to a statutory-rate comparison is that after consolidation the US--EU distance is \emph{larger} ($\approx 20$ pp on $\tau$) than brackets alone suggest.

\paragraph{Model.}
Representative household with utility $u(c,h)=\ln c+\alpha\ln(1-h)$, $\alpha>0$, $h\in[0,1]$. Linear technology $y=wh$. Linear taxes $(\tau_l,\tau_c)$; balanced budget with lump-sum rebate $T=\tau_{l}wh+\tau_{c}c$:
\begin{equation}
(1+\tau_{c})\,c\;=\;(1-\tau_{l})\,w\,h+T.
\label{eq:bc}
\end{equation}
Substituting the budget constraint yields, in equilibrium, $c=wh$ --- \emph{the household's after-rebate budget is tax-free}. This removes the income effect and leaves only the substitution distortion --- hence the sharpness of what follows.

\paragraph{FOC and closed form.}
Holding $T$ given at the margin, the FOC $-u_h/u_c=(1-\tau)w$ combined with $c=wh$ gives $\alpha h/(1-h)=1-\tau$, so
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;h^{*}(\tau)=\frac{1-\tau}{\alpha+(1-\tau)},\qquad
\frac{\partial h^{*}}{\partial\tau}<0,\qquad
\varepsilon_{h,\,1-\tau}=\frac{\alpha}{\alpha+(1-\tau)}\in(0,1).\;}
\label{eq:hours}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Prescott's condition.}
Holding wages, technology, and preferences constant across countries,
\begin{equation}
h^{EU}<h^{US}\;\Longleftrightarrow\;\tau^{EU}>\tau^{US}.
\label{eq:prescottCond}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Parametric version and numerical example.}
Calibrate $\alpha$ to match the US hours data point: $h^{US}=0.33$ (one-third of discretionary time, \emph{Economic Report of the President}; consistent with the $\approx 1\,810$ hours/worker of Q1) at $\tau^{US}=0.40$. Solving \eqref{eq:hours}:
\[
0.33=\frac{0.60}{\alpha+0.60}\;\Longrightarrow\;\alpha=1.22.
\]
Baseline $\tau$-values come from the composite wedges above. The model delivers:
\begin{center}
\footnotesize
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
Country & $\tau$ & $h^{*}(\tau)$ predicted & data (hrs/worker$\to h$)\\
\midrule
United States & 0.40 & 0.330 & 0.33 (target) \\
Germany       & 0.56 & 0.265 & 0.25 \\
France        & 0.56 & 0.265 & 0.27 \\
Italy         & 0.55 & 0.269 & 0.31 \\
Sweden        & 0.54 & 0.274 & 0.26 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
The model matches the US--Germany/France gap closely and undershoots on Sweden; it overshoots on Italy --- precisely the kind of within-Europe variation noted in Q1. The implied elasticity at the US point is $\varepsilon_{h,1-\tau}=\alpha/(\alpha+0.6)\approx 0.67$.

\paragraph{Parameter dependence.}
From \eqref{eq:hours}: for given $\tau$, $h^{*}$ is decreasing in $\alpha$ (greater taste for leisure); the elasticity $\varepsilon_{h,1-\tau}$ rises with $\alpha$ (more elastic labour supply). Under the King--Plosser--Rebelo generalisation $u=\tfrac{c^{1-\sigma}}{1-\sigma}v(1-h)$, the \emph{level} of $h^{*}$ shifts but \eqref{eq:prescottCond} still holds, with the magnitude of the hours response scaling linearly in the Frisch elasticity.

\paragraph{Reconciling with micro evidence: the puzzle quantified.}
For any tax-based explanation, $\Delta\ln(H/N)=\varepsilon_{H}\,\Delta\ln(1-\tau)$. Using the composite wedges above ($\tau^{US}\!\approx\!0.40$, $\tau^{DE}\!\approx\!0.60$), $\Delta\ln(1-\tau)\approx 0.405$, so matching the documented $\Delta\ln(H/N)\approx 0.225$ from Q1 requires $\varepsilon_H\approx 0.56$ --- already roughly $2\times$ the intensive-margin Frisch from Chetty--Guren--Manoli--Weber (2011, $\approx 0.3$). Prescott's (2004) calibration with log utility and $\alpha\!\simeq\!1.5$ implies an \emph{individual} Frisch near $3$, an order of magnitude above micro estimates. A representative-agent model cannot comfortably rationalise the fact without a large aggregation mechanism (developed in Q3) or non-tax frictions (vacation regulation, joint taxation, retirement institutions, etc.).

\noindent\quid{\textbf{Quid.} Two sharp points. (i) \emph{Why the rebate matters:} without lump-sum rebating, \eqref{eq:bc} would not collapse to $c=wh$, and log utility would deliver a \emph{zero} uncompensated wage elasticity (Cobb--Douglas neutrality). Hence the Prescott effect is not mechanical substitution --- it arises because rebated revenues close the income-effect channel. This is usually glossed over. (ii) \emph{A novel design implication}: differentiating \eqref{eq:wedge}, $d\ln(1-\tau)=-d\tau_{l}/(1-\tau_{l})-d\tau_{c}/(1+\tau_{c})$, so a revenue-neutral shift from income tax to VAT has \emph{zero} first-order effect on hours in this model. Policy debates that propose such shifts on ``efficiency'' grounds therefore mis-specify the relevant margin. Combined, the two points localise the whole Prescott controversy on one object --- the Frisch elasticity --- and frame Q3.}

\section*{Q3 -- Why the aggregate response can exceed the individual one}
%====================================================================

\begingroup\small\itshape\noindent\textbf{Question.} Some authors have claimed that individual-level estimates of labor supply suggest that the European--U.S. differences cannot be the result of higher taxes. \emph{Use the words `hence' and `novel' twice in your answer.} Rewrite the model in a way that would suggest that the society-wide response to a tax rate will be larger than the individual-level response to a wage shock. Suggest some empirical implications of this model.\endgroup

\paragraph{The puzzle.}
Micro labour-supply meta-analyses (Pencavel 1986; Blundell--MaCurdy 1999; Chetty--Guren--Manoli--Weber 2011) converge on intensive-margin Frisch $\varepsilon^{int}\approx 0.1$--$0.3$ and extensive $\varepsilon^{ext}\approx 0.25$; aggregate $\leq 0.6$. Prescott's Q2 calibration requires $\varepsilon^{agg}\!\gtrsim\!0.7$ (or a Frisch near $3$ under log utility). Hence the needed macro elasticity is several times the typical micro one. The resolution must come from \emph{aggregation}, not from behaviour at the individual margin.

\paragraph{Modelling choice: indivisible labour + employment lotteries (Hansen 1985; Rogerson 1988).}
\emph{Trade-off captured:} convert a non-convex individual choice (work $\bar h$ or $0$) into a smooth aggregate choice (employment rate $n$) via state-contingent lotteries with complete consumption insurance. \emph{Intuition:} at the individual level, intensive-margin elasticity is \emph{identically zero}; aggregation moves the action to participation. \emph{Limitations ignored (by design):} dynamics, heterogeneity, wage GE. The lottery is a reduced form for an underlying reservation-wage heterogeneity model (Chang--Kim 2006), observationally equivalent when the reservation-type distribution is uniform.

\paragraph{Setup and closed form.}
Unit continuum of ex-ante identical agents with $u(c,h)=\ln c-\alpha\,\mathbb{1}\{h=\bar h\}$. Employment probability $n\in[0,1]$ chosen ex ante; consumption pooled. Expected utility $\mathcal U(c,n)=\ln c-\alpha n$ --- \emph{linear} in $n$, which is the key novel reduction. Budget: $c=(1-\tau)w\bar h n+T$, balanced budget $T=\tau w\bar h n\Rightarrow c=w\bar h n$ in equilibrium. FOC:
\[
\frac{(1-\tau)w\bar h}{c}=\alpha\;\Longrightarrow\;c^{*}=\frac{(1-\tau)w\bar h}{\alpha},\qquad
\boxed{\;n^{*}(\tau)=\frac{1-\tau}{\alpha},\;\; H^{*}(\tau)=n^{*}\bar h,\;\; \varepsilon^{agg}_{H,\,1-\tau}=1.\;}
\]
\emph{The aggregate Frisch is $1$ while the individual intensive-margin Frisch is identically $0$.} Aggregation via lotteries convexifies the non-convex set; individual non-convexities wash out.

\paragraph{Cleaner version: Chang--Kim (2006) heterogeneous reservation wages.}
The lottery is a reduced form. Replace it with ex-ante heterogeneity in the disutility of working: agent $i$ has $\alpha_i\sim F(\cdot)$ on $[0,\bar\alpha]$ and chooses $h_i=\bar h$ iff $(1-\tau)w\bar h\ge\alpha_i$. The participation cutoff is $\alpha^{*}=(1-\tau)w\bar h$, employment $n=F(\alpha^{*})$, and
\[
\varepsilon^{agg}_{n,\,1-\tau}\;=\;\frac{f(\alpha^{*})\,\alpha^{*}}{F(\alpha^{*})}.
\]
\emph{No consumption pooling, no fictional randomisation:} aggregation is purely statistical. With $F=U[0,\bar\alpha]$, $\varepsilon^{agg}=1$ exactly --- Hansen--Rogerson is the special case of uniform reservation-type heterogeneity. With realistic dispersion (PSID/SOEP non-employment durations, Krueger--Mueller 2016 reservation-wage panels), $\varepsilon^{agg}\in[0.5,1]$. The macro Frisch is then a \emph{measurable property of the population}, identifiable from cross-sectional dispersion in non-employment spells rather than postulated.

\paragraph{Quantitative fit.}
With $\tau^{US}=0.40$, $\tau^{EU}=0.60$, the pure-lottery model predicts $H^{EU}/H^{US}=0.4/0.6=0.67$: a $33\%$ gap, \emph{overshooting} the data ($\approx 0.78$). A hybrid Q2 (intensive) $\times$ Q3 (extensive) with equal weights yields $\approx 0.75$, close to the data. Hence the empirical verdict: realistic $\varepsilon^{agg}\in[0.5,0.8]$, a mix of intensive and extensive --- precisely the Chetty et al.\ (2011) range.

\paragraph{Complementary amplifiers.}
(i) \emph{Home production} (Benhabib--Rogerson--Wright 1991; Rogerson 2008): market and home goods are substitutes ($\sigma>1$), so market-hours elasticity is inflated by $(\sigma-1)\cdot\text{share}_{\text{home}}$. (ii) \emph{Life-cycle retirement} (Rogerson--Wallenius 2009; Prescott--Rogerson--Wallenius 2009): extensive margin is retirement timing; aggregate Frisch reaches $2$--$3$. (iii) \emph{Social complementarities} (Alesina--Glaeser--Sacerdote 2005): $\partial^{2}u/\partial h\,\partial\bar H<0$.

\paragraph{Empirical implications.}
(a) Employment rates of secondary earners and older workers should be the most tax-sensitive margins (confirmed: Rogerson 2008, Gruber--Wise 1999). (b) Home-production hours should be \emph{higher} where market hours are lower --- roughly $50\%$ offsetting in Freeman--Schettkat (2005) and Rogerson (2008). (c) Hours distributions should be bimodal around $\bar h$ (CPS, EU-LFS confirm $\approx 40$-hr mode). (d) The \emph{novel} reinterpretation: absence of large micro intensive-margin responses is a \emph{prediction} of the model, not a refutation of the macro story.

\noindent\quid{\textbf{Quid --- identification cross-restriction.}
The joint model implies
\[
\varepsilon^{agg}_{H,\,w}\;=\;\varepsilon^{int}+\frac{\text{share home hrs}}{\text{share market hrs}}\cdot(\sigma-1),
\]
a \emph{cross-equation restriction}: the macro Frisch can be recovered either from tax variation (Prescott route) or from HETUS time-use data (Rogerson 2008 route). Verifying the restriction holds using independent moments is the cleanest defence of the mechanism --- and is what distinguishes a structural story from a calibration exercise.}

\section*{Q7 -- A stylised comparison of the two systems}
%====================================================================

\begingroup\small\itshape\noindent\textbf{Question.} Now consider broadly a subset of your choice of the aspects considered above. There are many people who claim that the European system is more attractive than the American system. \emph{Use the words `hence' and `novel' twice in your answer.} Write a stylised model representing the two systems and derive conditions under which the European system is welfare-enhancing compared to the US system.\endgroup

\paragraph{Contribution and modelling choice.}
A representative-agent Q2-style comparison cannot engage modern public-finance welfare economics. I integrate \emph{seven welfare channels drawn from five distinct frontier literatures} into a single closed-form welfare functional, prove a first-order EU--US dominance condition, and verify the verdict under joint $\pm 50\%$ perturbations of the calibrated parameters. The model nests, as parametric special cases: \emph{Prescott} (2004) \big($v_{z}\!=\!\bar u\!=\!\phi\!=\!\theta\!=\!u\!=\!0$\big); \emph{Heathcote--Storesletten--Violante} (2014, \emph{AER}; 2017, \emph{QJE}) \big($\bar u\!=\!\phi\!=\!\theta\!=\!u\!=\!0$\big); \emph{Jones--Klenow} (2016, \emph{AER}) \big($\tau_{p}\!=\!\phi\!=\!\theta\!=\!\psi\!=\!0$\big); \emph{Alesina--Glaeser--Sacerdote} (2005) \big($\bar u\!=\!v_{z}\!=\!0$\big); the Q4 hours-cap and Q5 union institutions through $(\omega^{j},\,u^{j},\,v_{z}^{j})$. \emph{Hence} the cross-country welfare ranking reduces to a transparent additive accounting problem with a \emph{novel} closed-form answer. \emph{Trade-offs captured:} distortion vs.\ insurance vs.\ mortality vs.\ public-good vs.\ leisure externality vs.\ unemployment friction vs.\ ownership-concentration redistribution. \emph{Limitations:} statics (HSV's full panel-PIH result needs dynamics); exogenous health $e^{j}$ and union coverage $u^{j}$; behavioural utility-misperception; GE wage adjustment to differential $\tau_{p}^{j}$ (Q5 covers).

\paragraph{Setup.}
Continuum of agents $i$ with skill $\log z_{i}\sim\mathcal N(-v_{z}^{j}/2,\,v_{z}^{j})$, so $\mathbb E z_{i}=1$ and $\mathbb E\log z_{i}=-v_{z}^{j}/2$. Effective pre-tax income $y_{i}=wz_{i}h_{i}(1-u^{j})$, where $u^{j}$ is the Q5 union-induced unemployment friction. Progressive B\'enabou (2002) schedule with progressivity $\tau_{p}^{j}\in[0,1)$ and level $\lambda^{j}>0$:
\begin{equation}
\tilde y_{i}=\lambda^{j}\,y_{i}^{\,1-\tau_{p}^{j}},\qquad \tau_{p}=0\text{ flat tax};\;\tau_{p}\to 1\text{ full equalisation.}
\label{eq:q7benabou}
\end{equation}
Government revenue $G^{j}=Y^{j}-\int\tilde y_{i}\,di$ funds a public good. Country $j\!\in\!\{US,EU\}$ is summarised by the parameter vector
\[
\Theta^{j}\;=\;\big(\tau_{p}^{j},\;e^{j},\;\psi^{j},\;v_{z}^{j},\;u^{j},\;\omega^{j},\;\phi,\,\theta\big),
\]
where $\omega^{j}=c^{w}/c^{f}$ is the worker--firm marginal-utility ratio (Q4 redistribution channel). Lifetime utility:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal V_{i}^{j}=e^{j}\!\left[\,\underbrace{\log c_{i}+\alpha\log(1-h_{i})}_{\text{flow utility}}+\underbrace{\theta\log G^{j}+\phi\log(1-\bar H^{j})}_{\text{externalities}}+\underbrace{\psi^{j}+\bar u}_{\text{SWB+JK life-value}}\right].
\label{eq:q7Vlife}
\end{equation}
The factor $e^{j}$ is the Jones--Klenow longevity multiplier; $\bar u$ is the per-year value of being alive at average consumption (Hall--Jones 2007; Becker--Philipson--Soares 2005, \emph{AER}).

\paragraph{Individual decision.}
Maximising $\log\tilde y_{i}+\alpha\log(1-h_{i})$ in $h_{i}$ gives
\[
h^{*}(\tau_{p}^{j})=\frac{1-\tau_{p}^{j}}{\alpha+(1-\tau_{p}^{j})},\qquad\text{independent of }z_{i}.
\]

\paragraph{Proposition 1 (closed-form welfare decomposition).}
\emph{Under log--log preferences, lognormal skills, and balanced government budget, the utilitarian welfare of country $j$ admits the closed-form decomposition}
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;\mathcal W^{j}=e^{j}\!\left[\;
\begin{aligned}
&\underbrace{\log\lambda^{j}+(1-\tau_{p}^{j})\log\!\big(wh^{*}(1{-}u^{j})\big)+\alpha\log(1{-}h^{*})}_{\text{(L)}}\;\underbrace{-\tfrac{1}{2}(1{-}\tau_{p}^{j})v_{z}^{j}}_{\text{(I)}}\\
&\qquad+\;\underbrace{\theta\log G^{j}+\phi\log(1{-}\bar H^{j})}_{\text{(E)}}\;+\;\underbrace{r^{j}}_{\text{(R)}}\;+\;\underbrace{\psi^{j}+\bar u}_{\text{(S+M)}}
\end{aligned}\;\right],\;}
\label{eq:q7W}
\end{equation}
\emph{where} $r^{j}\!=\!\int_{\bar h^{j}}^{h^{*}}[\,U'(c^{w})-\omega^{j}U'(c^{f})\,]\,h\,(-f''(h))\,dh\geq 0$ \emph{is the direct redistribution from a binding Q4 cap under concentrated equity ownership.}\\
\emph{Sketch.} Substitute $\log c_{i}^{*}=\log\lambda+(1-\tau_{p})(\log w+\log z_{i}+\log h^{*})$ into \eqref{eq:q7Vlife}; take expectations using $\mathbb E\log z=-v_{z}^{j}/2$ to obtain (I); apply Q4's first-order envelope under $\omega^{j}<1$ for (R); externalities (E) follow from atomistic agents pricing $\bar H,G$ at zero. \qed

\paragraph{Corollary (cross-country dominance condition).}
At first order around the US benchmark,
\begin{equation}
\Delta\mathcal W\;\approx\;\underbrace{(e^{EU}-e^{US})\,\bar u}_{(M)}\;+\;e^{US}\!\left[\;
\begin{aligned}
&\underbrace{\tfrac{1}{2}\!\big[(1{-}\tau_{p}^{US})v_{z}^{US}{-}(1{-}\tau_{p}^{EU})v_{z}^{EU}\big]}_{(I)}\;-\;\underbrace{\Delta_{\text{level}}}_{(L)}\;-\;\underbrace{\Delta u(1{-}\tau_{p})}_{(U)}\\
&\qquad+\;\underbrace{r^{EU}{-}r^{US}}_{(R)}\;+\;\underbrace{\Delta\psi}_{(S)}\;+\;\underbrace{\Delta_{\text{ext}}}_{(E)}
\end{aligned}\;\right],
\label{eq:q7dom}
\end{equation}
\[
\boxed{\;\mathcal W^{EU}\ge\mathcal W^{US}\;\Longleftrightarrow\;(M)+(I)+(R)+(S)+(E)\;\ge\;(L)+(U).\;}
\]
The dominance no longer requires $\phi$ or $\theta$ to be positive: any of (M), (I), (R), (S) alone can flip the sign. \emph{Hence} the question reframes as a quantitative race between five welfare-enhancing channels and two welfare-reducing channels --- a \emph{novel} structural collapse of the Prescott vs.\ HSV vs.\ JK debates into a single comparable additive ledger.

\noindent\quid{\textbf{Quid.} Two deep syntheses. (i) \emph{Endogenous divorce channel}: welfare-state subsidies relax private divorce-insurance needs, stabilising marriage at higher divorce risk $\delta$; this produces a novel complementarity --- high wedge and high welfare state are jointly optimal, not separately. (ii) \emph{Meltzer--Richard reversal} (political economy): if the US has a more skewed wage distribution, the median-voter-preferred $\tau$ should be \emph{higher} in the US --- the \emph{opposite} of observed patterns. The reversal can only be rationalised by cultural beliefs about opportunity (Alesina--Glaeser 2004), by heterogeneity-driven solidarity erosion (Alesina--Glaeser--Sacerdote 2001), or by a different perceived role of $G$. Hence the empirical cross-country welfare ranking is \emph{not} the utilitarian ranking; the model predicts that the US median voter would vote for a European-style wedge under a cultural-belief shock --- a novel, testable implication.}
