\begin{planbox}
\small\textbf{Plan and storyline.}
\textbf{Q1 -- there is an inconsistency in the data.} The US--EU
hours-per-worker gap is real (panels (a)/(b)) but its cross-country
relation to the tax wedge is loose: the 2023 OLS slope is signed
correctly ($-9.9$ hrs/pp) but $R^2\!=\!0.16$ and the residual SD is
$\approx 158$ hours -- one third of the entire DE--US gap (panel (c)).
Taxes account for some of the cross-country variation, not most of it.
\textbf{Q2 -- the elasticity needed to close the gap is larger than the
literature delivers.} The Prescott log--log model gives
$h^\ast(\tau)=(1-\tau)/(\alpha+1-\tau)$. Calibrating $\alpha$ to US
hours and matching the cross-country gap requires a Marshallian
elasticity of $\sim\!1$, while Chetty--Guren--Manoli--Weber (2011)
report a Hicksian intensive of $\sim\!0.30$. The gap between what the
model needs and what the micro evidence delivers is what we ask
\S 4--\S 6 to absorb -- something is constraining hours \emph{beyond}
the price of leisure.
\textbf{Q4 -- hours regulation:} a binding cap $\bar h$ is welfare-improving
iff $\bar h\!\in\!(2h^\text{soc}\!-\!h^\text{firm},\,h^\text{firm})$ in
the firm-power baseline; below $h^\ast$ it overshoots.
\textbf{Q5 -- single union:} bargaining weight $\eta$ delivers
$h(\eta)$ from $h^\text{firm}$ ($\eta\!=\!0$) to $h^\ast$ ($\eta\!=\!1$);
joint surplus is single-peaked at an interior $\eta^\text{soc}$ -- a
union can be welfare-improving or welfare-reducing depending on its
strength.
\textbf{Q6 -- culture:} a higher leisure preference $\alpha^{EU}$ shifts
$h^\ast$ down at every $\tau$; welfare-neutral by construction.
\textbf{Q7 -- welfare comparison and counterfactuals.} Shutting down EU's
cultural shift counterfactually adds $\Delta W\!=\!+0.088$; shutting
down its union channel \emph{removes} $\Delta W\!=\!-0.060$. Net
EU--US welfare difference at baseline is $-0.028$, real but
quantitatively second-order to non-labour channels (Jones--Klenow 2016).
Reproducible: \texttt{research/build\_figure\_panel.py},
\texttt{research/sim\_mechanisms.py}.
\end{planbox}

% =========================================================================
\section{Q1 -- The hours gap and its noisy relation to tax wedges}
% =========================================================================

\begingroup\small\itshape\noindent\textbf{Question 1.} Document the
difference between hours worked in the US vs Europe in recent years.
\endgroup

\subsection{Definitions (used throughout the problem)}
Three working-age (15--64) quantities, common notation:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{$HE$} $=$ \emph{hours per employed worker} (annual hours
actually worked; OECD \texttt{DSD\_HW}). The intensive object the
labour-supply model speaks to.
\item \textbf{$e\!\equiv\!E/N$} $=$ \emph{employment rate} (fraction of
the working-age population that is employed; OECD \texttt{DSD\_LFS}).
\item \textbf{$H/N\!\equiv\!HE\cdot e$} $=$ \emph{hours per adult}
(total labour input per working-age person).
\end{itemize}
The body of the answer focuses on $HE$ -- the object that responds to
the price of leisure in the static labour-supply model of Q2 and the
firm--worker interaction model of Q4--Q6. The participation/extensive
margin $e$ is documented in this section but not analytically pursued
in the body.\footnote{\label{fn:extensive}\textbf{Why we omit the
extensive margin from the body.} Cross-country differences in $e$ are
substantial (US 71\% vs IT 59\% in 2019, BBF 2019 Tab.\ 2) and
matter for total labour input $H/N$. We do not model them because
(i) the participation decision is shaped by joint-vs-individual income
tax filing, public childcare, employment-protection legislation and
the structure of the welfare system -- a separate body of theory to
the labour-supply model that Q2 asks for; (ii) layering a participation
margin on top of the firm-profit-max framework of Q4--Q6 would dilute
the analytic focus of those questions without changing their
qualitative welfare conditions; (iii) the question prompt asks
specifically about \emph{hours worked}, which the OECD reports
unambiguously as $HE$. The relevant numerical evidence is documented
in \texttt{research/repl\_bbf\_decomp.py} for the interested reader.}

\subsection{The fact}
\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figure_panel.pdf}
\caption{\scriptsize\textbf{Three-panel evidence.}
(a) Hours per worker, percent deviation from US, 1970--2024 (OECD;
DE/FR/IT/ES/NL/SE/UK; US baseline at zero).
(b) Effective labour wedge $\tau_h$, percent deviation from US,
1970--2007 (McDaniel 2011 national-accounts measure).
(c) Composite tax wedge $\tau$ vs hours per worker, 2023 cross-section
($n\!=\!19$ OECD countries); OLS line and $\pm 1\sigma_\text{resid}$
band.
\emph{Source / replication:} \texttt{research/build\_figure\_panel.py}.}
\label{fig:gapPanel}
\end{figure}

Three readings of Fig.~\ref{fig:gapPanel}.

\textbf{(i) Hours fell in every focus country relative to the US since
1970 (panel a).} Northern Europe (DE, NL) is now $\approx -25$\% to
$-30$\% below the US line; Southern Europe (IT, ES) at $-10$\% to
$-15$\%. The gap widened steadily; it is not a recent phenomenon.

\textbf{(ii) The labour wedge rose in every focus country relative to
the US (panel b).} Sweden is $+120$\% above the US wedge in 2007;
DE/FR/IT are $+60$ to $+70$\%. The two series move in opposite
directions, qualitatively consistent with a tax-driven story.

\textbf{(iii) The 2023 cross-section is signed correctly but very
noisy (panel c).} OLS slope $\hat\beta\!=\!-9.9$ hrs/pp,
$r\!=\!-0.40$, $R^2\!=\!0.16$. The residual SD is $\approx 158$ hours.
To put it in scale: the entire DE--US hours gap is $470$ hours, so the
residual SD is roughly one third of the gap I am trying to explain.
Italy ($\tau\!=\!45.1$\%) and Greece ($\tau\!=\!36.7$\%) work
\emph{more} hours per worker than France ($\tau\!=\!46.8$\%); the
four highest-$\tau$ countries (BE, DE, AT, FR) span $\approx 200$
hours within a 6-pp wedge band.

\subsection{The inconsistency the rest of the problem is asked to
explain}
The within-country time path (panels a/b) is consistent with a
tax-driven story; the cross-country cross-section (panel c) is not.
Either the elasticity that fits the time series is much larger than
the cross-section delivers (the Prescott--CGM puzzle, Q2), or the
cross-section is corrupted by country-specific institutional and
cultural frictions that the tax wedge does not capture (Q4--Q6). Q2
formalises the first horn; \S 4--\S 6 build the second.

\section{Q2 -- Prescott model and the elasticity that would be needed}
% =========================================================================

\begingroup\small\itshape\noindent\textbf{Question 2.} Summarise the
main differences in income tax between US and Europe. Write a model
of labour supply and derive formally the conditions under which the
European tax system leads to fewer hours worked. \emph{Use the words
`hence' and `novel' twice.}\endgroup

\subsection{Modelling choice and its trade-offs}
\textbf{Trade-off captured.} I want a closed-form expression for
$h^\ast(\tau)$ and a clean elasticity comparative static, so I can
compare the model's prediction to the cross-section in
panel~(c) of Fig.~\ref{fig:gapPanel} and to the micro estimates in the
elasticity literature. Log--log preferences with a balanced lump-sum
rebate are the standard choice (Prescott 2004; Cooley--Prescott 1995):
they give a one-parameter ($\alpha$) closed-form labour-supply
function and a Marshallian elasticity that is monotone in $\alpha$.
\textbf{What I am ignoring (and why).} (i) The intertemporal margin: a
static model has no Frisch elasticity, so I cannot speak to the
business-cycle volatility of hours. This is fine because the question
is about long-run cross-country differences, not high-frequency
adjustments. (ii) A non-balanced budget: I assume the government
rebates the full tax revenue lump-sum; relaxing this changes the income
effect but not the substitution effect, leaving the qualitative
comparative static intact. (iii) Capital and savings: log preferences
in a static model imply consumption equals labour income. With
positive savings the relevant wedge would be the wedge on consumption
\emph{plus} the wedge on capital income; for prime-age workers near
the median the capital channel is small (CGM 2011 \S 3) so the
omission is not first-order. (iv) Heterogeneity: the representative
household assumption hides distributional effects, which I address in
Q4 (heterogeneous workers under a uniform cap).

\subsection{The relevant tax object: the composite wedge}
Workers supply labour only to consume; \emph{hence} a consumption tax
is isomorphic to a labour tax in any static model with no untaxed
savings. The relevant object is the composite labour wedge,
\begin{equation}
\tau\;\equiv\;1\;-\;\frac{1-\tau_l}{1+\tau_c},
\qquad
(1-\tau)\;=\;\frac{1-\tau_l}{1+\tau_c},
\label{eq:wedge}
\end{equation}
where $\tau_l$ is the OECD employee + employer labour-tax wedge and
$\tau_c$ is the standard VAT/sales rate. After consolidation (panel
(c) of Fig.~\ref{fig:gapPanel}): US $\tau\!\approx\!0.35$ (federal
income tax + payroll + state sales averaging), DE/FR/IT/SE
$\tau\!\approx\!0.54$--$0.56$. The \emph{novel} accounting observation
relative to a statutory-rate comparison is that consolidating the
labour-side and consumption-side wedges puts the EU--US distance at
$\approx 20$\,pp, materially larger than a top-bracket comparison
alone suggests.

\subsection{The simplest one-margin model}
A representative household has log--log preferences over consumption
and leisure,
\[
u(c,h)\;=\;\log c\;+\;\alpha\log(1-h),
\qquad \alpha>0,\;\;h\in[0,1],
\]
faces wage $w$, linear labour tax $\tau_l$, consumption tax $\tau_c$,
and lump-sum rebate $T$. The budget $(1+\tau_c)c=(1-\tau_l)wh+T$
collapses to $c=wh$ in the symmetric equilibrium $T=\tau_l wh+\tau_c c$.
The first-order condition $-u_h/u_c=(1-\tau)w$ with $c=wh$ gives the
worker's preferred hours as a closed-form function of $\tau$:
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;h^\ast(\tau)\;=\;\frac{1-\tau}{\alpha+1-\tau},
\qquad
\frac{\partial h^\ast}{\partial\tau}<0,
\qquad
\varepsilon^M_{h,1-\tau}\;=\;\frac{\alpha}{\alpha+1-\tau}\in(0,1).\;}
\label{eq:prescott}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Prescott condition (sufficient).}
$h^\ast(\tau^{EU})<h^\ast(\tau^{US})\Longleftrightarrow\tau^{EU}>\tau^{US}$.
The one-margin model translates the cross-section sign of panel~(c)
into a structural prediction with a single free parameter ($\alpha$).

\subsection{Parametric version, anchor, and what it would take to fit
the cross-section}
\textbf{Anchor.} Anchoring $h^{US}\!=\!0.33$ at $\tau^{US}\!=\!0.35$
(the BLS-OECD ratio of weekly hours-worked to total awake hours)
inverts \eqref{eq:prescott} to give
$\alpha=(1-\tau)(1-h^\ast)/h^\ast=1.32$ (Prescott 2004 uses a similar
calibration). The implied Marshallian intensive elasticity at the US
point is $\varepsilon^M=\alpha/(\alpha+1-\tau)=0.67$.

\textbf{What the model needs to fit panel (c).} The Germany--US
hours gap of $-25\%$ at $\Delta\tau=+21$\,pp implies
$\partial\log h/\partial\tau\approx -1.2$, equivalent to
$\varepsilon^M\!\approx\!1$. The literature reports much smaller numbers
(see below). The cross-section is asking for an elasticity the model
cannot deliver under standard preferences -- this is the inconsistency
flagged in the planbox.

\textbf{Elasticity taxonomy} (Chetty--Guren--Manoli--Weber 2011, AER
P\&P 101(3); Hansen 1985, JME 16(3); Rogerson 1988, JME 21(1)):
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Marshallian intensive (uncompensated):} CGM (2011)
report $\approx 0.30$ for prime-age men, $\approx 0.50$ for married
women.
\item \textbf{Hicksian intensive (compensated):} CGM (2011) report
$\approx 0.30$ -- the figure on which micro and macro estimates
broadly agree.
\item \textbf{Frisch:} $\approx 1$ in representative-agent macro
(Hansen 1985), larger under indivisible labour (Rogerson 1988); this
is the elasticity Prescott (2004) requires to fit the cross-country
panel.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Where this leaves us.} \emph{Hence} the gap between
panel-(c)'s implied $\varepsilon^M\!\approx\!1$ and CGM's
$\varepsilon^M\!\approx\!0.30$ is the substantive content of the
Prescott controversy: the model's elasticity is too low to fit the
cross-section, and the micro literature is closer to $0.30$ than
to $1$. The \emph{novel} feature of \S 4--\S 6 is to absorb this gap
through three institutional/cultural frictions, each disciplined by
a closed-form welfare condition rather than left as a verbal
residual.\footnote{\label{fn:contracts}\textbf{Other frictions we are
not modelling.} The model also abstracts from contract stickiness:
in many European countries, weekly hours per worker are set in a
sectoral collective contract that is not renegotiated for years
even as $\tau$ changes. A worker who would, on the margin, prefer
fewer (or more) hours cannot adjust because the contract is not
written that way. This is a real friction documented in Cahuc--Carcillo
(2014, \emph{IZA WP}) and in the EU Labour Force Survey, but it is
econometrically equivalent (in our static framework) to a binding
hours cap, and is therefore captured implicitly by the cap mechanism
of \S 4.}

\section{Common framework for Q4--Q5--Q7}
% =========================================================================

The setup that follows is shared by \S 4 (regulation), \S 5 (unions),
and \S 6 (culture); \S 7 reads off the welfare comparison. The
framework is the simplest closed-form quadratic--linear structure that
admits all three mechanisms in one diagram and lets each
\emph{optimality condition} be derived analytically.

\subsection{Modelling choice: trade-offs and limits}
\textbf{Trade-off captured.} I want closed-form expressions for
\emph{both} the worker's and the firm's preferred hours, and a
welfare condition that is one-line and sign-determinate. A
quadratic-disutility worker with linear consumption and a
linear-quadratic firm-profit function gives these. Any concavity in
the firm's production captures the diminishing returns to extra hours
on the same fixed factor; any convexity in the worker's disutility
captures the increasing marginal cost of work as leisure shrinks.
\textbf{What I am ignoring.} (i) Heterogeneous workers, addressed
explicitly at the end of \S 4. (ii) Sectoral composition and the
role of the public sector in setting reference hours.\footnote{\label{fn:public}\textbf{Sectoral composition.}
Larger public sectors in Europe imply more workers on contracts that
are sticky for political-economy reasons unrelated to the firm-profit
maximisation problem we model. France's public-sector employment
share (\textit{secteur publique élargi}) is $\approx 22\%$, vs $\approx 16\%$ in the US.
Public-sector hours decisions are
governed by a different mechanism -- collective bargaining over
service provision rather than firm--worker bargaining -- which we do
not model. The labour-supply effects we identify in \S 4--\S 5 should
therefore be read as applying to the private-sector portion of the
labour market.}
(iii) General equilibrium feedbacks of $h$ on $w$: in our partial
equilibrium $w$ is taken as fixed.

\subsection{Worker, firm, and joint surplus}
\textbf{Worker payoff} (linear consumption, quadratic disutility):
\[
V_w(h;\,\alpha,\tau)\;=\;\underbrace{(1-\tau)wh}_{\text{net income, }y\cdot h}\;-\;\tfrac{\alpha}{2}h^2,
\qquad
h^\ast(\tau,\alpha)\;\equiv\;\arg\max_h V_w\;=\;\frac{(1-\tau)w}{\alpha}.
\]
The first term is the worker's net income $y\cdot h$ at fixed wage $y\!\equiv\!(1-\tau)w$; the second is leisure-cost.

\textbf{Firm payoff} (linear-quadratic per worker):
\[
V_f(h;\,A,\beta)\;=\;\underbrace{Ah\;-\;\tfrac{\beta}{2}h^2}_{\text{revenue}}\;-\;\underbrace{wh}_{\text{wage bill}},
\qquad
h^\text{firm}(A,\beta,w)\;\equiv\;\arg\max_h V_f\;=\;\frac{A-w}{\beta}.
\]

\textbf{Joint surplus.} $W(h)\!\equiv\!V_w(h)+V_f(h)$ is concave with
maximum at
\[
h^\text{soc}(\tau,\alpha,A,\beta,w)\;=\;\frac{(1-\tau)w+(A-w)}{\alpha+\beta}.
\]

\subsection{Baseline calibration}
$A=2$, $w=1$, $\beta=2$, $\alpha=2$, $\tau=0.4$, giving:
\[
h^\ast=0.30,\qquad h^\text{soc}=0.40,\qquad h^\text{firm}=0.50.
\]
\textbf{Justification.} (i) $\tau=0.4$ sits between the US and EU
2023 composite wedges, the regime where the EU--US distance is
qualitative (panel (c) of Fig.~\ref{fig:gapPanel}). (ii) $\alpha$ and
$\beta$ at the same value ($=2$) is the symmetric benchmark; the
welfare-improving region of the cap then simplifies to $\bar h\!\in\!(h^\ast,h^\text{firm})$,
which makes the optimality condition transparent. The qualitative
results are invariant to varying $\alpha,\beta$ separately (verified
in \texttt{sim\_mechanisms.py}). (iii) $A=2,w=1$ gives $h^\text{firm}=0.5$,
within the realistic empirical range ($\sim 1{,}500$--$2{,}000$ hours
per year mapped to a fraction of total annual time endowment). The
absolute level is not load-bearing; only the orderings
$h^\ast<h^\text{soc}<h^\text{firm}$ matter.

\subsection{Why the same $\tau$ can deliver different equilibrium $h$
in US and EU}
Under perfect competitive Walrasian clearing, both economies pick
$h=h^\ast(\tau)$. Under firm-power bargaining (the baseline of \S 4--\S
5), both economies pick $h=h^\text{firm}$. Neither extreme produces a
US--EU hours gap at common $\tau$ and common preferences. Three
institutional / cultural channels break the symmetry:
\begin{itemize}
\item[\textbf{(i)}] \textbf{Hours regulation (\S 4).} A government cap
$\bar h$ binds at $\bar h<h^\text{firm}$, shifting equilibrium to
$\bar h$.
\item[\textbf{(ii)}] \textbf{Unions (\S 5).} A single union with
bargaining weight $\eta$ pulls $h$ continuously from $h^\text{firm}$
($\eta\!=\!0$) toward $h^\ast$ ($\eta\!=\!1$).
\item[\textbf{(iii)}] \textbf{Culture (\S 6).} A higher leisure
preference $\alpha^{EU}>\alpha^{US}$ shifts $h^\ast(\tau,\alpha)$ down
at every $\tau$.
\end{itemize}

\section{Q4 -- Hours regulation: the optimality condition for a cap}
% =========================================================================

\begingroup\small\itshape\noindent\textbf{Question 4.} Labour-market
regulation limiting hours. Welfare effects on workers and firms?
\endgroup

\subsection{Modelling choice}
\textbf{Trade-off captured.} A cap is a quantity instrument; its
welfare effect depends on whether it sits above or below the joint
surplus optimum $h^\text{soc}$. The Pigouvian-tax analogue is exact: a
cap at $h^\text{soc}$ implements the joint-surplus optimum, a cap
above is non-binding, a cap below overshoots in the same way an
over-Pigouvian tax does.
\textbf{What I ignore.} (i) Enforcement costs and the incentive for
informal-sector evasion. (ii) Heterogeneity in $\alpha$ (treated at
the end of this section). (iii) The general equilibrium response of
$w$ when many firms face the cap (Estev\~ao--S\'a 2008 finds a partial
$\Delta w$ pass-through but treats it separately).

\subsection{Setup: the firm-power baseline}
In the absence of regulation the firm picks $h\!=\!h^\text{firm}$ and
the worker accepts. The government imposes cap $\bar h$. The
constrained equilibrium is
\[
h^{eq}(\bar h)\;=\;\min\bigl\{\bar h,\;h^\text{firm}\bigr\}.
\]

\subsection{Optimality condition (Q4 result)}
\textbf{Claim.} Total surplus $W(h)$ is strictly concave and peaks at
$h^\text{soc}$. Comparing the constrained equilibrium to the no-cap
baseline:
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;\Delta W(\bar h)\;\equiv\;W(\bar h)-W(h^\text{firm})
\;\;\begin{cases}
=0 & \text{if } \bar h\geq h^\text{firm}\\
>0 & \text{iff } \bar h\in\bigl(2h^\text{soc}-h^\text{firm},\,h^\text{firm}\bigr)\\
=0 & \text{at }\bar h=2h^\text{soc}-h^\text{firm}\\
<0 & \text{if } \bar h<2h^\text{soc}-h^\text{firm}.
\end{cases}\;}
\label{eq:capwelf}
\end{equation}
\textbf{Optimality.} The cap maximises joint welfare at
$\bar h=h^\text{soc}$, where $\Delta W$ achieves the value
$\frac{1}{2}(\alpha+\beta)(h^\text{firm}-h^\text{soc})^2$ above the
firm-power baseline.
\textbf{At $\alpha=\beta$} (our baseline), $2h^\text{soc}-h^\text{firm}=h^\ast$,
so the welfare-improving region simplifies to
$\bar h\in(h^\ast,h^\text{firm})$.
\textbf{Proof.} $W(h)$ is quadratic in $h$ with negative second
derivative $-(\alpha+\beta)$, so $\Delta W(\bar h)=W(\bar h)-W(h^\text{firm})$
is concave with the same root structure as a parabola through
$h^\text{soc}$ -- one root at $h^\text{firm}$ (cap doesn't bind) and a
mirror root at $2h^\text{soc}-h^\text{firm}$. $\square$

\subsection{Numerical simulation}
\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{sim_cap.pdf}
\caption{\scriptsize Hours-cap simulation, baseline parameters
$A\!=\!2,w\!=\!1,\beta\!=\!2,\alpha\!=\!2,\tau\!=\!0.4$ giving
$h^\ast\!=\!0.30$, $h^\text{soc}\!=\!0.40$, $h^\text{firm}\!=\!0.50$.
\textbf{Panel (a)} -- primitive functions $V_w(h)$, $V_f(h)$, $W(h)$
over the full hours domain, marking each function's interior maximum.
\textbf{Panel (b)} -- surplus components along the constrained
equilibrium $h^{eq}(\bar h)=\min\{\bar h,h^\text{firm}\}$.
\textbf{Panel (c)} -- $\Delta W(\bar h)$ vs the no-cap baseline; green
band is the welfare-improving region of \eqref{eq:capwelf}; red region
is the overshoot. Maximum gain $\Delta W=0.020$ at $\bar h=h^\text{soc}=0.40$.
\emph{Source:} \texttt{research/sim\_mechanisms.py}.}
\label{fig:simcap}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Empirical anchor and US--EU mapping}
The 35-hour Aubry reform (France 1998--2000) is the canonical anchor.
Estev\~ao--S\'a (2008, \emph{JEEA} 6(5)) and Chemin--Wasmer (2009,
\emph{JLE} 27(4)) report a $\approx 4\%$ aggregate hours decline in
covered sectors with near-zero employment effect and partial wage
compensation. Mapping to the model: at the baseline,
$\bar h^{Aubry}\!\approx\!0.875\cdot h^\text{firm}\!=\!0.4375$ -- well
inside the welfare-improving band of \eqref{eq:capwelf} (above
$h^\text{soc}\!=\!0.40$). The US has no equivalent binding cap (the
FLSA 40-hour overtime threshold rarely binds at the median margin).
This generates a US--EU hours gap at common $\tau$ that the Prescott
model of \S 2 cannot see.

\subsection{Heterogeneous workers}
With worker types indexed by $\alpha_i$ (the leisure-preference
parameter of \S 6), the welfare-improving band of \eqref{eq:capwelf}
becomes type-specific: a single national cap $\bar h$ helps high-$\alpha$
workers (low $h^\ast_i$, $\bar h$ sits comfortably above their
preferred level) and hurts low-$\alpha$ workers (high $h^\ast_i$,
possibly above $\bar h$). The cap therefore redistributes from
low-$\alpha$ to high-$\alpha$ workers. The \emph{average} effect is
positive iff the population is predominantly high-$\alpha$ at the
binding margin -- roughly, a country whose workforce on average values
leisure highly will benefit from a binding cap, while a country whose
workforce values income highly will lose.

\section{Q5 -- Single union and the bargaining channel}
% =========================================================================

\begingroup\small\itshape\noindent\textbf{Question 5.} Unions in the
economy and their response to sectoral shifts. Show the impact of
sectoral shifts on hours; how does the response change in a unionised
economy. \emph{Use the words `hence' and `novel' twice.}\endgroup

\subsection{Modelling choice}
\textbf{Trade-off captured.} A union with bargaining weight $\eta$
internalises the trade-off between firm profits and worker welfare
endogenously, in contrast to the cap of \S 4 which is set
exogenously by the government. This is the Coase analogue: a Coasian
side-payment achieves the joint optimum without the planner needing
to know firm technology, provided property rights are well defined and
the union has the right bargaining power. In our setup the union
\emph{is} the bargaining mechanism.
\textbf{What I ignore.} (i) Inter-union competition (the user's
framing: a single industry-wide union); (ii) The wage as a
bargaining variable (right-to-manage models; here $h$ is the
bargained variable, $w$ is fixed); (iii) Endogenous union formation
itself.

\subsection{One union, dues share, alignment with workers}
A single industry-wide union, all workers inside, each paying a
dues-share $s\in(0,1)$ of net labour income. The union's payoff is
\[
\text{Union}\;=\;s\cdot V_w(h;\,\alpha,\tau)
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\arg\max_h\,(\text{Union})\;=\;\arg\max_h V_w\;=\;h^\ast.
\]
The dues share scales the union's objective but does not move its
preferred $h$. \emph{Hence} the union and its workers are perfectly
aligned -- there is no agency problem, in contrast to a model where
the union maximises dues revenue $s\cdot wh$ (which would push $h$
toward $h^\text{firm}$, in the opposite direction). This is the
analytically clean case the user asked for.

\subsection{Bargaining over $h$ at fixed wage}
The union and the firm bargain over $h$ at the given wage $w$. We use
a weighted-utilitarian Nash-style criterion -- \emph{novel} relative
to the right-to-manage tradition where wage is the bargained variable
and employment is set unilaterally; here $h$ is bargained directly:
\begin{equation}
h(\eta)\;=\;\arg\max_h\,(1-\eta)V_f(h)+\eta V_w(h)
\;=\;\frac{(1-\eta)(A-w)+\eta(1-\tau)w}{(1-\eta)\beta+\eta\alpha},
\label{eq:nashh}
\end{equation}
where $\eta\in[0,1]$ is the worker's bargaining weight. By
construction
\[
h(0)=h^\text{firm},\qquad h(1)=h^\ast.
\]
The union drags equilibrium hours from the firm's preferred level
toward the worker's interior optimum, continuously in $\eta$.

\subsection{Optimality condition (Q5 result)}
\textbf{Claim.} Joint surplus $W(\eta)\!=\!V_w(h(\eta))+V_f(h(\eta))$
is concave in $\eta$ with a unique interior maximum at the value
$\eta^\text{soc}$ for which $h(\eta^\text{soc})=h^\text{soc}$.

\textbf{At baseline $\alpha\!=\!\beta\!=\!2$:}
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;\eta^\text{soc}\;=\;\frac{1}{2},\qquad
h(\eta^\text{soc})=h^\text{soc}=0.40,\qquad
\Delta W(\eta^\text{soc})=W(0.40)-W(0.50)=0.02.\;}
\label{eq:unionopt}
\end{equation}
\textbf{Welfare-improving region.}
$\Delta W(\eta)>0\iff\eta\in(0,\,2\eta^\text{soc})=(0,\,1)$ at
baseline; outside this interval (here, only at $\eta=1$ exactly)
the union over-bargains and total welfare drops back to the no-union
level. \emph{Hence} the welfare ranking of the bargaining channel is
not monotone in $\eta$: a moderately strong union helps; a maximally
strong union returns to the worker's individual optimum, which is
\emph{not} the joint optimum.\footnote{At general $\alpha,\beta$ the
condition becomes $\eta<1\!-\!\frac{\beta(h^\ast-h^\text{firm})/h^\text{firm}}{(\alpha h^\ast - \beta h^\text{firm})/h^\text{firm}}$; the
qualitative interior-peak structure is preserved.}

\subsection{Sectoral shocks (the original Q5 question)}
A mean-preserving shock to sectoral productivity $A_j$, $j\in\{A,B\}$,
under firm-power bargaining ($\eta\!=\!0$) raises the cross-sector
hours dispersion in proportion to the shock to $A_j$ via
$h^\text{firm}_j=(A_j-w)/\beta$. Under union-driven bargaining
($\eta\!\to\!1$), the union pulls each sector toward the same
worker-side $h^\ast(\tau,\alpha)$, which does \emph{not} depend on
$A_j$, so cross-sector hours dispersion is compressed. The
\emph{novel} observation here is the welfare structure of this
compression: it has an insurance gain (under log preferences,
reducing wage/hours variance at fixed mean is welfare-improving by
$\frac{1}{2}\Delta v_z$) and an allocative loss (high-$A$ sectors
operate below their first-best $h^\text{firm}_j$). The
Blanchard--Wolfers (2000) reduced-form interaction
($\hat\beta=0.32$, s.e.\ $0.10$) is a partial identification of
which dominates: the rich-country sample average suggests insurance
dominates, consistent with the welfare-improving band of \eqref{eq:unionopt}.

\subsection{US--EU mapping}
Bargaining-coverage rates (OECD ICTWSS 2022): US $\approx 10\%$, IT
$\approx 80\%$, FR $\approx 95\%$, SE $\approx 90\%$. The
order-of-magnitude difference in coverage is consistent with a
several-fold difference in $\eta$, which the model translates into a
mechanically-determined hours gap at common $\tau$. The headline
falsifiable implication: cross-country hours should covary more
strongly with bargaining \emph{coverage} than with $\tau$ alone --
an empirical check we leave for future work.

\section{Culture: a downward shift in $h^\ast(\tau,\alpha)$}
% =========================================================================

\subsection{Modelling choice}
\textbf{Trade-off captured.} The cultural channel is a preference
shifter, not an institutional friction: a higher leisure-cost
parameter $\alpha$ moves the worker's reaction function down at every
$\tau$. The choice of \emph{vertical shift in the preferences function}
is the simplest possible specification that captures ``Europeans
value leisure more.''
\textbf{What I ignore.} (i) The endogenous-preference question (does
the union channel of \S 5 cause $\alpha^{EU}\!>\!\alpha^{US}$ to
emerge over time? Alesina--Glaeser--Sacerdote 2005 argue yes); (ii)
heterogeneity within Europe (the cultural shifter is treated as a
single value $\alpha^{EU}$); (iii) reference-group dynamics
(Bowles 1998, \emph{JEL} 36(1)).

\subsection{Setup}
\[
V_w^{EU}(h)\;=\;(1-\tau)wh\;-\;\tfrac{\alpha^{EU}}{2}h^2,
\qquad
\alpha^{EU}\!=\!2.7,\;\;\alpha^{US}\!=\!2.0.
\]
Comparative statics:
\[
h^\ast(\tau,\alpha^{EU})\;=\;\frac{(1-\tau)w}{\alpha^{EU}}\;<\;h^\ast(\tau,\alpha^{US})
\quad\text{for every }\tau,
\qquad
\frac{\partial h^\ast}{\partial\alpha}\;=\;-\frac{(1-\tau)w}{\alpha^2}\;<\;0.
\]

\subsection{Why $\alpha^{EU}>\alpha^{US}$? Two structural candidates}
\textbf{(a) Alesina--Glaeser--Sacerdote (2005, \emph{NBER Macro Annual}
20).} Leisure is a complementary good: agents enjoy time off more
when their neighbours are also off. After post-WWII union-driven
shorter-week reforms in Europe, the coordination equilibrium of the
leisure-complementarity model shifted European preferences toward
shorter weeks. The same mechanism did not operate in the US because
no equivalent aggregate union shock occurred. Under this reading,
the union channel of \S 5 is the upstream cause of the cultural
channel of \S 6.

\textbf{(b) Reference-group / habit formation.} If utility depends on
$h-h^{\text{ref}}$ where $h^{\text{ref}}$ is the average hours in the
worker's reference group, low-$h$ reference groups generate low
individual $h$ choices, sustaining the low-$h$ reference -- a
self-fulfilling equilibrium with the same observable implication.

\subsection{Numerical simulation}
\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{sim_culture.pdf}
\caption{\scriptsize Cultural channel. \textbf{(a)}
$h^\ast(\tau,\alpha)\!=\!(1-\tau)w/\alpha$ for two values of $\alpha$;
shaded band is the culture-induced gap
$\Delta h^\ast(\tau)=[1/\alpha^{US}-1/\alpha^{EU}](1-\tau)w$. At

\label{fig:simculture}

\subsection{Welfare implication}
The cultural channel is observationally similar to the union channel
of \S 5 (both push $h$ down at common $\tau$) but differs sharply in
welfare. A high-$\alpha$ EU equilibrium is welfare-optimal \emph{in
the EU's own preferences}: the worker is at $h^\ast(\tau,\alpha^{EU})$,
their own interior optimum, and there is no cross-country Pareto
ranking because the two economies have different preferences. The
clean discriminating evidence -- not pursued here -- is whether
immigrant workers from low-$h$ countries import home-country leisure
preferences (cultural channel) or assimilate to host-country
bargaining institutions (union channel).

\section{Q7 -- Welfare comparison and counterfactuals}
% =========================================================================

\begingroup\small\itshape\noindent\textbf{Question 7.} Write a stylised
model representing the two systems and derive the conditions under
which the European system is welfare-enhancing compared to the US
system. \emph{Use the words `hence' and `novel' twice.}\endgroup

\subsection{Modelling choice and limits}
\textbf{Trade-off captured.} The welfare comparison wants to isolate
how each of the three frictions (\S 4 cap, \S 5 union, \S 6 culture)
contributes to a US--EU welfare differential. A counterfactual
experiment that shuts down each channel one at a time gives
\emph{additive} welfare deltas, in the spirit of Jones--Klenow (2016).
\textbf{What I ignore.} (i) Channels that are not labour-market
mechanisms (life expectancy, aggregate consumption, inequality), each
of which Jones--Klenow (2016) shows is quantitatively larger than the
labour-market gap. (ii) Endogenous interactions between channels
(\S 6 may be the equilibrium of the union game of \S 5); we treat
$\alpha^{EU}$ as exogenous in the body. (iii) Distributional weights:
total surplus is treated utilitarian, equal weight on $V_w$ and $V_f$.

\subsection{The four cases}
At common $\tau$, the EU equilibrium can sit below the US for one of
four reasons, each with a distinct welfare answer:

\textbf{(I)} US at $h^\text{firm}$ (firm-power), EU at $h^\text{soc}$
or nearby via binding cap or moderate union. \textbf{EU dominates:}
$W^{EU}\!=\!W(h^\text{soc})>W(h^\text{firm})\!=\!W^{US}$. Sufficient
condition: institutional bargain places equilibrium hours in
$(2h^\text{soc}-h^\text{firm},\,h^\text{firm})$.

\textbf{(II)} US at $h^\text{firm}$, EU below $h^\ast$ via overshoot
cap or excessive union. \textbf{EU is welfare-decreasing:}
$W^{EU}<W^{US}$ if $\bar h<2h^\text{soc}-h^\text{firm}$ or
$\eta>2\eta^\text{soc}$. Sign-determinate from \S 4--\S 5.

\textbf{(III)} Same institutions, $\alpha^{EU}>\alpha^{US}$. Both
economies are at their own first-best
$h^\ast(\tau,\alpha)$. \emph{Hence} the cultural channel is
welfare-neutral by construction.

\textbf{(IV) Compounded.} Institutions and culture both differ. The
EU lies on $h(\eta^{EU};\alpha^{EU})$ for $\eta^{EU}>\eta^{US}$ and
$\alpha^{EU}>\alpha^{US}$. Condition for $W^{EU}\geq W^{US}$:
\[
h(\eta^{EU};\alpha^{EU})\;\in\;\bigl(2h^\text{soc}(\alpha^{EU})-h^\text{firm},\;h^\text{firm}\bigr),
\]
i.e.\ EU's compounded equilibrium must lie in the welfare-improving
band defined relative to the EU's own $h^\text{soc}$.

\subsection{Counterfactual decomposition: the Q7 simulation}
The user-requested experiment: take the EU equilibrium and shut
down each channel sequentially. Starting from US baseline
($\eta\!=\!0$, $\alpha\!=\!\alpha^{US}$, no cap), apply the EU's
cultural shifter, then add the EU's union strength.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{sim_q7.pdf}
\caption{\scriptsize Q7 welfare frontier and counterfactual
decomposition. \textbf{(a)} $W(\eta;\alpha)$ for two values of
$\alpha$: red curve is the US-culture welfare frontier, green curve
is the EU-culture frontier. The US equilibrium (\,$\eta\!=\!0$,
$\alpha\!=\!2.0$, $W\!=\!0.300$\,) and EU equilibrium
(\,$\eta\!=\!0.5$, $\alpha\!=\!2.7$, $W\!=\!0.272$\,) are marked.
Counterfactuals: CF1 shuts down EU's union ($\eta\!=\!0$) keeping
EU culture, dropping $W$ to $0.212$; CF2 shifts EU's culture to US
($\alpha\!=\!2.0$) keeping EU's union, raising $W$ to $0.320$.
\textbf{(b)} US$\to$EU waterfall: applying the cultural shift alone
costs $\Delta W\!=\!-0.088$ (red); adding the union recovers
$\Delta W\!=\!+0.060$ (green); net EU--US gap $=-0.028$.
\emph{Source:} \texttt{research/sim\_mechanisms.py}.}
\label{fig:simq7}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Reading the counterfactuals}
The decomposition delivers a sharp \emph{novel} structural finding:
the union channel is welfare-creating in the EU, contrary to a naive
reading that ``unions reduce hours therefore cost welfare.'' The
union does reduce hours, but at $\alpha^{EU}\!=\!2.7$ the firm-power
equilibrium $h^\text{firm}\!=\!0.5$ is far above the EU worker's
optimum $h^\ast_{EU}\!=\!0.222$, so the union pulls $h$ \emph{toward}
the joint optimum. The cultural shift alone (CF1: union off, EU
culture on) destroys $W$ by $0.088$ relative to the US; the union
in EU recovers $0.060$ of that, leaving a net EU--US gap of
$-0.028$.

\subsection{Magnitudes}
At baseline parameters and the institutional bargain
$\eta^{EU}\!=\!0.5$ vs $\eta^{US}\!=\!0$,
\[
W^{EU}-W^{US}\;=\;0.272-0.300\;=\;-0.028
\]
in the units of the simulation -- approximately $1\%$ of total surplus
at the social optimum. Jones--Klenow (2016) report a US--EU
welfare gap on the order of $-10$ to $-25\%$ in consumption-equivalent
units across all channels (consumption, leisure, life expectancy,
inequality). Mortality and aggregate consumption levels carry the bulk
of this; the labour-market block analysed here is sign-determinate
but quantitatively second-order relative to demographic and TFP
channels.

\subsection{Verdict}
\begin{verdictgreen}
\small\textbf{Q7 verdict.} Europe welfare-dominates the US through the
labour-market mechanism iff the institutional bargain (cap level or
union strength) places equilibrium hours in the band
$(h^\text{soc}(\alpha^{EU}),h^\text{firm})$ where the constraint is
binding but not overshoot. \emph{Hence} a moderate Aubry-style cap
(binding but not below $h^\ast$) and Continental-coverage unionisation
($\eta\!\sim\!0.5$, near $\eta^\text{soc}$) deliver a welfare gain
relative to the firm-power baseline; very-low-$\eta$ Anglo
unionisation ($\eta\!<\!0.1$) or hyper-aggressive caps below $h^\ast$
deliver a welfare loss. The cultural channel is welfare-neutral by
construction. The novel quantitative summary from
Fig.~\ref{fig:simq7}: the cultural shift alone costs $\Delta W=-0.088$
relative to the US; the union channel recovers $+0.060$ of that loss;
net EU welfare disadvantage is $\approx 1\%$ of total surplus. The
dominant US--EU welfare differences -- in the Jones--Klenow (2016)
decomposition -- come from non-labour channels (life expectancy,
aggregate consumption, inequality) that this analysis does not
address.
\end{verdictgreen}
